# Research Memo: AC Interconnection as a Pathway to Market Integration — Concrete Examples for an HVDC-Preserves-Autonomy Argument

**Prepared for:** Policy essay on U.S. interregional transmission, HVDC, and public-power autonomy
**Date:** May 17, 2026

## TL;DR

- **The cleanest, best-documented case for the thesis is ERCOT.** FERC’s own orders (Tres Amigas, 130 FERC ¶ 61,207; Southern Cross, 147 FERC ¶ 61,113; AEP Energy Partners, 164 FERC ¶ 61,056) explicitly treat asynchronous HVDC ties as the technical–legal predicate for preserving ERCOT’s exemption from Federal Power Act §201 jurisdiction, while warning that synchronous AC commingling would collapse that exemption. ERCOT is the only context where the “AC = integration / DC = autonomy” argument has been articulated on the record by the regulator itself.
- **In the Eastern Interconnection, three episodes operationally demonstrate the pattern:** (1) Entergy’s December 2013 entry into MISO South, where SPP, TVA, and FERC dockets documented that AC interconnection density (14,000 MW SPP–Entergy capacity; 40% of MISO-Entergy flows looping over TVA) made standalone operation untenable; (2) the October 2015 integration of the WAPA-Basin Electric-Heartland Integrated System into SPP, where decades of jointly-operated AC transmission was the explicit structural argument for full RTO membership; and (3) the now-13-year-running PJM-MISO seam dispute (epitomized by NIPSCO’s 2013 complaint in EL13-88), where AC interconnection density at NIPSCO’s tie points has chronically produced loop-flow and modeling problems that the Joint Operating Agreement cannot solve.
- **The counterexamples sharpen the thesis:** BPA’s May 9, 2025 choice of SPP Markets+ over CAISO’s EDAM — despite its own consultant’s finding of $69–221 million/year in foregone benefits — was justified to preserve governance autonomy despite AC synchrony with California; Xcel’s April 2018 withdrawal from Mountain West/SPP integration was framed as a refusal to accept RTO membership pressure; and TVA, Southern Company, and the Southeast generally have used SERTP/SEEM precisely to manage AC-interconnection coordination *without* RTO consolidation. The pressure is real, consciously resisted, and the resistance is costly — which is exactly why HVDC’s structural autonomy-preservation matters.

## Key Findings

**Finding 1 — ERCOT is the textbook case.** FERC has repeatedly held that ERCOT and Texas utilities remain exempt from FPA §201 “public utility” jurisdiction *only because* their interconnections to the Eastern and Western grids are asynchronous DC ties authorized under FPA §§210–212, which contain a statutory carve-out (16 U.S.C. §824i(c), §824j(b)) providing that orders under those sections shall not subject the recipient to public-utility jurisdiction. FERC’s May 15, 2014 Southern Cross order (147 FERC ¶ 61,113, Dockets TX11-1-000/001) directly affirmed that the HVDC tie order would not “cause ERCOT, Oncor, CenterPoint, or any other ERCOT utility or other entity that is not already a public utility to become a ‘public utility,’ as that term is defined by section 201 of the Federal Power Act … and subject to the jurisdiction of the Commission for any purpose other than for the purpose of carrying out the provisions of sections 210 and 211 of the Federal Power Act.”  In its July 26, 2018 AEP Energy Partners order (164 FERC ¶ 61,056, Docket TX18-1-000),  FERC warned in dicta that planned new HVDC projects and Mexico’s planned synchronous reconnection of Baja “could separately result in interstate power flows … between either Arizona or California and ERCOT,” potentially triggering full §201 jurisdiction. Then-Texas PUC Chair DeAnn Walker responded with public memos directing that ERCOT modify protocols so that it “will not authorize transactions across the DC ties into Mexico in the event that the jurisdiction of ERCOT is at risk,” and asserting that “ERCOT has the authority to require the breakers to the transmission lines that could cause any such transactions to potentially occur [to be opened under the same circumstance].” This is the canonical articulation of the AC-vs-DC autonomy thesis, made by both the federal regulator and a state regulator on the record.

**Finding 2 — Entergy’s 2013 entry into MISO South was operationally driven by dense AC interconnection.** The structural argument was implicit, not explicit, but is fully documented in FERC dockets EC12-145 and related proceedings. SPP’s January 22, 2013 protest documented that SPP was “14 times better connected with Entergy” than MISO was — “14,000 MWs of physical capability over multiple interconnections”  — and that MISO’s planned 4,000 MW of North-to-South dispatch would induce loop flows of approximately 30% across the SPP grid (with only ~8% over the direct MISO-Entergy contractual path). TVA’s protest stated that “approximately 40 percent of Entergy-MISO flows will flow over the TVA system, overloading between 12 and 70 transmission facilities.” The AC interconnection density made standalone operation increasingly indefensible. After Entergy had withdrawn from SPP in 1997 and tried (and failed) to join SPP as RTO (2000-2001), Grid South (2001), and SeTrans (2002-2003), and after operating under FERC-imposed Independent Coordinator of Transmission oversight from 2006, Entergy’s state regulators (LA PSC May 2012, AR/TX October 2012, MS/New Orleans November 2012) ultimately found MISO membership in the public interest. Entergy joined December 19, 2013. According to Entergy’s own December 19, 2013 press release, “MISO membership is projected to save Entergy’s customers $1.4 billion during the first decade alone.”  The *physical predicate* for those savings was the pre-existing AC interconnection that produced the inefficient loop flows in the first place.

**Finding 3 — The 2015 Integrated System → SPP integration is the clearest “AC drove the merger” case in the Eastern Interconnection.** WAPA-Upper Great Plains, Basin Electric, and Heartland Consumers Power District had jointly operated transmission as a single physically-integrated AC system since 1977 (originally the Joint Transmission System, then the Integrated System under a 1998 agreement) in eastern Montana, the Dakotas, and adjacent areas. When FERC Orders 888/889 made bilateral JTS arrangements untenable and the IS partners began evaluating alternatives in 2001, the explicit conclusion (announced January 2014, consummated October 1, 2015) was that the existing AC integration argued for joining SPP rather than continuing standalone or joining MISO. WAPA became the first federal power marketing administration in an RTO.  Per Fitch Ratings’ August 26, 2016 press release citing SPP’s own analysis, “SPP estimates benefits totaling $334.1 million over a 10-year period for its members from the inclusion of the IS. Notably, WAPA is the first federal agency to join a regional transmission organization.”  The structural argument was made explicitly by Basin Electric: the existing AC transmission system was already operated on “an integrated single-system basis,” and tariff/governance unification was the formalization of that physical reality.

**Finding 4 — The PJM-MISO seam (2004-present) is the running example of AC-density creating chronic pressure for consolidation.** NIPSCO’s September 13, 2013 complaint at FERC (Docket EL13-88)  directly attacked the structural problem: the PJM-MISO Joint Operating Agreement had produced almost no interregional transmission projects despite massive loop flows. NIPSCO documented 5,334 MVA of ties to PJM’s ComEd and 2,824 MVA to PJM’s AEP  — sandwiched in MISO between two PJM islands — with significant through flows from PJM economic dispatch overloading lines never designed for those transfers.  NIPSCO’s 2015 FERC testimony documented a 22,179 MW difference in modeled generation capacity between MISO and PJM models for the same 2024-2025 timeframe.  FERC’s April 21, 2016 order partially granted NIPSCO’s complaint,  directing MISO and PJM to align models, lower the interregional project voltage threshold to 100 kV, and tighten the JOA. M2M (market-to-market) settlement payments continue at tens of millions of dollars annually but interregional transmission projects remain rare. Academic and advocacy literature (ACORE 2021; Sustainable FERC Project 2016; NARUC 2015 Seams Primer) has consistently argued that the underlying problem is structural: two different markets sharing dense AC transmission cannot fully optimize,  and the “split the savings” approach of JOAs/M2M is a partial workaround for the absence of consolidation. The explicit “merger is the answer” argument has been made by clean energy advocates but rejected by both RTOs and their states.

**Finding 5 — The counterexamples (BPA 2025, Xcel 2018, TVA, Southern) confirm the AC-pressure dynamic is real because utilities consciously act, and pay, to resist it.** BPA’s May 9, 2025 Day-Ahead Market Policy chose SPP’s Markets+ over CAISO’s EDAM despite analysis by Energy + Environmental Economics (E3), presented at BPA’s Workshop 9 on November 4, 2024, showing $69–221 million per year in higher production cost from Markets+.  BPA’s stated rationale: “independent governance — meaning that the market is not obligated to any single state, entity, or trade association — is paramount to its participation in a day-ahead market.”  BPA chose a smaller market footprint specifically to avoid being absorbed into California’s regulatory orbit, even though it is AC-synchronous with the rest of the Western Interconnection. Xcel’s April 20, 2018 withdrawal from Mountain West/SPP integration was framed by former Montana commissioner Travis Kavulla in unusually candid terms: “It probably is a sense that when you join an RTO, you go from being a big fish in a small pond to a small fish in a big pond. … As a utility in a particular state jurisdiction if I exercise huge suasion in my legislature and state regulatory process I’m going to be pretty innately skeptical to a much more multilateral, multi-state process regulated by people not in my state House but Washington D.C.”  TVA has resisted RTO membership for decades despite AC interconnections with PJM, MISO, SERC, and Southern Company. The January 2025 ACORE-SREA report (*Recommendations for Reducing Costs and Improving Reliability for Tennessee Valley Authority Customers*) explicitly argues that “Congress [should] consider enacting legislation to eliminate the [TVA] Fence and enable utilities in the region to access alternative sources of supply.” Southern Company’s similarly-rejected GridSouth (2001) and SeTrans (2002) RTO proposals, replaced by the much weaker Southeast Energy Exchange Market (SEEM) in 2020, show the Southeast’s deliberate strategy of using bilateral AC coordination mechanisms specifically to avoid RTO consolidation. 

## Details

### 1. ERCOT and the FPA §201 Jurisdictional Argument (Strongest Case)

**Legal mechanism.** FPA §201(b)(1) limits FERC’s plenary jurisdiction to “transmission of electric energy in interstate commerce.” Under *FPC v. Florida Power & Light Co.*, 404 U.S. 453 (1972), synchronous AC interconnection that “commingles” in-state and interstate power converts the entire flow into interstate commerce, triggering §201 jurisdiction.  ERCOT’s exemption rests on (a) its asynchronous DC ties not creating commingling, and (b) FERC’s prior orders authorizing those ties pursuant to FPA §§210–212, which contain an express statutory carve-out (16 U.S.C. §824i(c), §824j(b)) preserving public-utility non-jurisdiction.

**Tres Amigas (2010), 130 FERC ¶ 61,207 (Docket EL10-22-000).** Tres Amigas LLC, formed by former PJM CEO Phil Harris,  proposed an HVDC SuperStation in Clovis, NM to interconnect all three U.S. interconnections via VSC-HVDC converters. Tres Amigas sought a prospective declaratory order disclaiming FERC jurisdiction over ERCOT utilities that would interconnect to the hub. FERC *refused* the generalized disclaimer, holding that Tres Amigas had not sufficiently identified which ERCOT entities would connect and encouraging the use of the FPA §§210/211 procedural vehicle instead. This is critical: FERC’s refusal shows that DC technology *alone* does not categorically preserve the ERCOT exemption — the §§210–212 statutory carve-out is what does the work. The project was never built; the Eastern Interconnection partner (Xcel/SPS) dropped out in 2015.

**Southern Cross / Southern Spirit HVDC (2014), 147 FERC ¶ 61,113 (Dockets TX11-1-000, TX11-1-001).** Pattern Energy’s proposed 320-mile, 3 GW HVDC line from Rusk County, TX to Choctaw County, MS. FERC’s May 15, 2014 order directed Garland Power & Light, Oncor, and CenterPoint to interconnect and provide transmission, while expressly affirming non-§201 jurisdiction (verbatim quote in Finding 1). This is the cleanest single articulation of the AC/DC jurisdictional principle in FERC’s docket. The U.S. Department of Energy awarded the project $360 million from the Bipartisan Infrastructure Law in October 2024.

**AEP Energy Partners (2018), 164 FERC ¶ 61,056 (Docket TX18-1-000).** FERC authorized transmission services in/out of ERCOT to Mexico over the Sharyland, Eagle Pass, and Laredo DC ties under §211 only — explicitly *not* under §201. But FERC then warned in dicta that planned new HVDC projects (Nogales, Arizona, 300 MW) plus Mexico’s proposed 1.5 GW DC line synchronously reconnecting Baja California with the mainland Mexican grid “could separately result in interstate power flows … between either Arizona or California and ERCOT” via commingling in Mexico. This was the moment FERC publicly acknowledged that the ERCOT exemption is fragile and depends on the continued absence of synchronous AC pathways anywhere in the loop.

**Texas response (2018-2019).** PUCT Chair DeAnn Walker’s public memos in PUCT Docket Control Number 48540 framed the autonomy-preservation strategy concretely: market participants must obtain “necessary FERC disclaimers” and ERCOT must physically be able to open breakers to prevent jurisdictionally-risky transactions. 

**Niskanen Center summary (2024).** “In 2014, FERC concluded that the proposed Southern Spirit HVDC line… would not trigger federal jurisdiction under 201(b). Because the two AC grids would remain asynchronous, linked only through controllable converter stations with scheduled power transfers, FERC didn’t consider ERCOT utilities to be transmitting electricity in interstate commerce, preserving their exemption from federal oversight. By contrast, the proposed but never constructed Tres Amigas project would have linked all three interconnects through an HVDC hub… FERC rejected that request, finding that the hub would have effectively integrated ERCOT into interstate commerce by enabling multiregional power flows and market interaction, despite HVDC’s asynchronous operation.”

### 2. Entergy → MISO South (December 19, 2013)

Entergy operated as a four-state standalone (LA, AR, MS, TX) after withdrawing from SPP in 1997. SPP’s protest at FERC (Docket EC12-145, January 22, 2013) and TVA’s parallel filing both made the operational case in unmistakable physical terms: AC interconnection density between Entergy and SPP was massive (14,000 MW), and any market dispatch decisions by MISO would force loop flows across non-MISO systems. The structural argument made in state regulatory proceedings was efficiency and cost — Entergy’s own December 19, 2013 press release stated “MISO membership is projected to save Entergy’s customers $1.4 billion during the first decade alone”  — but the *physical predicate* for those savings was the pre-existing AC interconnection that produced the inefficient loop flows in the first place. The pattern: AC interconnection makes operational coordination unavoidable; the question is only whether that coordination happens through bilateral arrangements (which proved unstable from 1997-2013) or through formal RTO membership.

### 3. WAPA-Basin-Heartland Integrated System → SPP (October 1, 2015)

The Integrated System was a formally jointly-operated transmission system among WAPA-UGP, Basin Electric, and Heartland under a 1998 agreement (successor to the 1977 Joint Transmission System).  It functioned as a single AC operating system in eastern Montana, the Dakotas, and adjacent areas. After studying SPP, MISO, and standalone options from 2001 onward, the IS partners concluded in January 2014 that SPP membership offered the best benefits.  SPP estimated $334.1 million in net benefits over a decade for existing SPP members from the integration. WAPA became the first federal power marketing administration to join an RTO.  This is the most explicit “AC integration drove the RTO decision” case in U.S. history: the partners had been operating their AC system on an “integrated single-system basis” for decades, and joining SPP was the formalization of that integration under a common tariff. Basin Electric’s own historical narrative on its website acknowledges the JTS framework “enabled Basin Electric to lease capacity on the federal transmission system and deliver output from its first power plant to members throughout the region by building only 12 miles of high-voltage transmission line.” 

### 4. PJM-MISO Seam (2004-present)

NIPSCO’s September 13, 2013 complaint at FERC (Docket EL13-88) attacked the structural problem head-on. NIPSCO documented:

- 5,334 MVA of ties to PJM’s ComEd plus 2,824 MVA to PJM’s AEP  — total ~8 GW of AC interconnection
- “Significant congestion exists on our transmission system which was never designed for these types of PJM transfers” 
- A 22,179 MW difference in modeled generation capacity between MISO and PJM models for the same 2024-2025 timeframe 
- Failure of the JOA’s interregional planning to produce projects: from 2008-2015, MISO had approved only three Market Efficiency Projects totaling $87.5M (0.5% of all MTEP investment) despite over $6 billion in real-time congestion 

FERC’s April 21, 2016 order partially granted NIPSCO’s complaint, directing MISO and PJM to align models, lower the interregional project voltage threshold to 100 kV, and tighten the JOA. M2M settlement payments between PJM and MISO continue at tens of millions of dollars annually (2014 MISO M2M payments: $62.6M; FFE value: $132M)  but few interregional transmission projects have been built. Academic and advocacy literature (ACORE 2021; Sustainable FERC Project 2016; NARUC 2015 Seams Primer) has argued that the underlying problem is structural: dense AC interconnection between two different market structures cannot be fully optimized through coordination mechanisms alone.  As one NARUC commenter put it, “Good coordination cannot overcome bad market design.”  The explicit “merger is the answer” argument has been made by clean energy advocates but rejected by both RTOs and their states.

### 5. Counterexamples (Autonomy-Preserving Resistance)

**BPA / Markets+ vs. EDAM (May 9, 2025).** BPA’s Day-Ahead Market Policy chose Markets+ despite the E3 Workshop 9 analysis (November 4, 2024) showing $69–221 million/year higher production cost than EDAM.  BPA’s stated reason was governance independence. The Pacific Northwest is AC-synchronous with California; BPA’s choice deliberately accepts a market seam where there is no physical seam, sacrificing efficiency to preserve autonomy. This is the contemporary mirror image of the ERCOT argument: where ERCOT uses physical asynchrony to preserve regulatory autonomy, BPA uses *market* asynchrony (different RTO/market footprints despite AC synchrony) to preserve governance autonomy. Critics including Seattle City Light and the NW Energy Coalition explicitly argued that BPA was choosing the worse market for governance reasons;  BPA explicitly conceded the trade-off in its Final Policy.

**Xcel / Mountain West (April 20, 2018).** SPP’s March 2018 analysis estimated $270 million–$1.024 billion in net benefits to *existing SPP members*  (not MWTG members) from MWTG integration. Xcel withdrew citing “limited benefits for the company’s 1.4 million customers in Colorado; lack of market expansion opportunities for the MWTG; and increasing uncertainty over the costs.”  Travis Kavulla’s “big fish in a small pond” formulation is the explicit autonomy-preservation argument.

**TVA / Southeast.** TVA has AC ties to MISO, PJM (via AEP), SERC neighbors, and Southern Company, and has resisted RTO membership for decades. The January 2025 ACORE-SREA report calls for legislation to eliminate the TVA “Fence” and require RTO membership — the academic/policy articulation of the argument that AC interconnection density *should* push TVA to RTO membership, made explicitly but politically rejected. Southern Company’s similarly-rejected GridSouth (2001) and SeTrans (2002) proposals, replaced by SEEM (2020), show the Southeast’s deliberate strategy of using bilateral AC coordination mechanisms specifically to avoid RTO consolidation.  Southern’s filings to FERC and DOE in 2023-2024 (per Balch & Bingham) have continued to argue that transmission consolidation would “infringe on state’s rights.” 

### 6. Order 1000 and the Implicit Assumption

FERC Order 1000 (2011) required regional and interregional transmission planning processes and cost allocation methodologies.   The implicit assumption was that paired regions could plan interregional AC transmission together — but Order 1000 did *not* require either region to actually build joint projects  or coordinate market structures. Wilson & Zarnikau (2020, *Electricity Journal*) and LBNL’s Order 1000 implementation reviews both document near-total failure to produce interregional Order 1000 projects. The structural finding: AC interregional planning cannot work well between dissimilar market structures. The unstated argument is that Order 1000’s vision presupposed market harmonization that never materialized — which itself supports the user’s thesis that AC interconnection without market integration produces dysfunctional outcomes.

### 7. NEPOOL/NYISO (Northeast Historical Precedent)

Per the New England Power Pool’s own website (“About NEPOOL”): “1971 – In response to the great Northeast blackout of 1965, the New England Power Pool (‘NEPOOL’) was formed to coordinate transmission planning and to achieve economic and reliability benefits through coordinated regional dispatch of power.”  Per the Sustainable FERC Project (“Navigating ISO-NE”): “On November 9, 1965, an interruption in a single transmission line set in motion the Great Northeast Blackout, resulting in 30 million customers across 8 states losing power.”  The blackout originated at a 230-kV protective relay near the Sir Adam Beck Hydroelectric Power Station No. 2 in Queenston, Ontario,  and cascaded synchronously across the Eastern Interconnection. The Northeast Power Coordinating Council (1966),  New York Power Pool, and NEPOOL all emerged directly from the demonstration that AC synchronous interconnection produces cascading-failure risk that only joint dispatch can manage. ISO-NE (1997)  and NYISO (1999) inherited these structures and formalized them as RTOs after FERC Order 888. This is the classical historical case: AC interconnection-driven cascading risk produced joint dispatch, joint dispatch produced power pools, power pools produced ISOs/RTOs. It is the cleanest historical articulation of the thesis, although couched in reliability rather than market-integration terms.

## Recommendations

For the policy essay:

1. **Lead with ERCOT.** It is the single example where the regulator itself has articulated the AC-creates-jurisdictional-integration argument on the record. Use the Southern Cross order quote (147 FERC ¶ 61,113) as the canonical citation. Note that FERC’s refusal of the Tres Amigas generalized disclaimer (130 FERC ¶ 61,207) shows the safe harbor is procedural (§§210–212) rather than purely technological — but asynchrony is the necessary technical predicate.
1. **Use the Integrated System (WAPA-Basin-Heartland → SPP) as the strongest constructive example.** It is the cleanest case of pre-existing physical AC integration formally producing tariff/governance integration, with the partners making the argument explicitly. WAPA being a federal agency with statutory mission, like TVA, makes the analogy directly usable.
1. **Use Entergy-MISO and PJM-MISO seam as implicit-argument cases.** SPP’s 14,000 MW protest filing and NIPSCO’s 8 GW of ties to PJM are concrete numbers that show how AC density creates loop-flow problems that push toward consolidation, even when the explicit argument is not made by the parties themselves.
1. **Use BPA-Markets+ as the contemporary parallel to the TVA argument.** BPA’s choice of a *measurably less efficient* market (E3 quantified the cost: $69–221M/year) specifically to preserve governance independence demonstrates that the AC-pressure dynamic is real, consciously resisted, and *expensive*. For TVA’s audience, this is the most direct contemporary precedent for why structural autonomy preservation can be worth its operational cost.
1. **Note the historical depth.** The NEPOOL/Northeast blackout precedent shows the pattern is at least 60 years old: AC interconnection without market coordination produces reliability and economic problems that ultimately drive consolidation. The thesis is not novel; it is the dominant structural force in U.S. transmission history.
1. **Flag the limits.** The argument is not deterministic — TVA, Southern, and Xcel have all successfully resisted AC-driven consolidation for decades. The pressure is real but not inevitable. HVDC preserves a *choice* about whether to integrate; AC quietly constrains that choice by raising the cost of standalone operation.

**Benchmarks that would change the recommendation:** If a future FERC order under §§210–212 were to *narrow* the public-utility safe harbor (the AEP Energy Partners 2018 dicta hint at this), the HVDC autonomy argument would weaken, and TVA-type entities would need affirmative statutory protection (analogous to the §201(f) federal-agency exemption TVA already enjoys). If Markets+ and EDAM unify (as the Pathways Initiative might produce by 2027), BPA’s autonomy strategy becomes moot and the argument shifts to whether market-design-level autonomy is achievable without footprint separation.

## Caveats

- The verbatim FERC order excerpts in this memo (Southern Cross 147 FERC ¶ 61,113; AEP Energy Partners 164 FERC ¶ 61,056; Tres Amigas 130 FERC ¶ 61,207) are drawn from law-firm client alerts, FERC press summaries, and Niskanen Center analysis that quote the underlying orders. Before publication, the user should pull the actual FERC order PDFs from FERC eLibrary (elibrary.ferc.gov) using these docket numbers to verify exact wording. The Troutman/Pepper alert dated August 1, 2018 appears to provide the most reliable direct quotation of the 164 FERC ¶ 61,056 “interstate power flows” passage.
- The “AC creates pressure for integration” argument is made *explicitly* only in the ERCOT context. In every other case (Entergy, IS-SPP, PJM-MISO seam, NEPOOL), the argument is implicit in the factual record but not articulated as a general principle by the parties. The user’s policy essay would be making a synthesizing argument from these implicit cases — which is defensible but should be acknowledged as synthesis rather than presented as if the parties themselves had said so.
- BPA’s May 2025 decision is contested and may be revisited; the Northwest political and energy-market environment remains fluid through 2026-2027 as Markets+ implementation proceeds.
- The TVA “Fence” arguments and SACE/ACORE reform proposals are advocacy positions, not consensus regulatory or academic views. Citing them as “the argument that has been made” is accurate; citing them as authoritative would not be.
- Throughout, the user should be careful to distinguish *physical* AC interconnection pressure (which is structural and reasonably durable) from *market-design* pressure (which depends on FERC rules and could change). HVDC neutralizes the physical pressure; only statutory protection neutralizes the market-design pressure.
