# Federal Anchor Transmission: Interregional Reliability and the Layered Reserve Fund

*Companion document to “Federal Anchor Transmission: A Proposal for Interregional Buildout Through Federally-Built Clean Firm Generation.”*

*Fred Stafford · [fred@publicpowerreview.org](mailto:fred@publicpowerreview.org)*
*Version 1.1 · May 18, 2026*

This companion document develops the cost recovery architecture for the federal anchor program’s interregional reliability infrastructure. The starting point is the question of how to size the anchor lines so they actually deliver interregional reliability value — a question that requires reconciling the public power proponent’s concerns about ratepayer protection and structural autonomy with the interregional transmission proponent’s concerns about meaningful new infrastructure at scale. The analytical journey works through what the lines actually buy for reliability (which is more bounded than classical interregional transmission framing suggests), how cost recovery should be structured if the lines are sized to deliver bidirectional reliability capability, and what financing architecture matches cost incidence to benefit incidence across the program’s various constituencies.

The endpoint is a generalized layered beneficiary-pays cost recovery architecture that applies the same structural logic to both nuclear construction cost overruns and reliability vintage capacity payments. Every party with a structural interest in the program’s success — the publicly-owned utility acquirer, the destination investor-owned utility, the hyperscaler offtaker, and the federal government — pays a defined share of both layers, with the share calibrated to that party’s share of benefit. The Nuclear Construction Reserve Fund described in the main proposal expands to become a Federal Anchor Program Reserve Fund covering both cost overrun absorption and reliability vintage capacity payments, with hyperscaler contributions as the principal funding source and federal appropriations bounded to tail risk that no specific beneficiary funds alone.

The architectural innovation is that the program’s financing structure becomes systematically aligned with its benefit structure. No single party bears the full cost of any layer; no single party gets fully insulated from the program’s costs. Each constituency has skin in the game on each layer, with the magnitude of contribution reflecting the magnitude of benefit. This is structurally more honest and politically more defensible than any of the single-payer alternatives we considered along the way.

## Executive Summary

- **The anchor lines’ contribution to interregional reliability is more bounded than classical transmission framing suggests.** The federal anchor plant delivers some fixed nameplate output; the line moves that output between TVA territory and the destination region but doesn’t generate power itself. During grid stress events, the line’s bidirectional dispatch capability allows TVA to reclaim some of the contracted flow for in-territory use, or to import emergency capacity from the destination RTO, but these flows are not additive to total system generation — they reallocate existing capacity rather than producing new capacity.
- **The most consequential reliability benefits are in-territory generation and the destination region’s contracted offtake**, both of which exist whether or not the anchor lines are built. The transmission-specific reliability benefit emerges during asymmetric stress events (when one region is stressed and the other isn’t), where geographic diversity allows utilities to lean on each other’s non-stressed conditions. This benefit is real but bounded to roughly 300-500 MW of bidirectional emergency exchange capability per line.
- **Building this bidirectional capability into the lines requires sizing them above contracted demand**, with a defined reliability vintage of 300-500 MW on top of the contracted vintage. This is meaningfully smaller than the fleet-scale sizing that pure interregional transmission framing might support, but meaningfully larger than the right-sized sizing that pure contracted-demand framing produces.
- **The cost recovery problem for the reliability vintage is acute under straightforward rate-basing.** Each segment owner (TVA on its home-jurisdiction segment, destination IOU on its AC leg and minority converter station stake) builds infrastructure sized for the full physical capacity including reliability vintage but only the contracted vintage produces commercial revenue. Standard cost-of-service rate-basing would load the reliability vintage carrying cost onto contracted vintage customers, creating exactly the cross-subsidy problem that vintaged rate-basing was designed to prevent.
- **Three structural approaches to fixing the cost recovery problem are available**: (1) federal appropriations cover the full reliability vintage carrying cost across all segment owners through a federally-administered capacity payment mechanism; (2) the line is sized to contracted vintage only with bidirectional dispatch capability operating within that envelope; (3) the federal authority owns parallel reliability vintage infrastructure across all segments, which is structurally clean but practically difficult given institutional and regulatory constraints.
- **Approach (1) is structurally a federally-administered, federally-funded capacity market** — the federal authority is the single buyer, the asset owners are the recipients, the federally-defined pricing methodology calibrates payments to cost-of-service recovery or avoided-alternative-cost benchmarks. This works but requires sustained federal appropriations of $300-450 million per year across the four Phase 1 pilots.
- **The hyperscaler-funded alternative** expands the Nuclear Construction Reserve Fund framework to cover the reliability vintage capacity payments through hyperscaler per-MWh contributions on federally-anchored offtake. This is structurally cleaner than pure federal appropriations because it ties funding to the beneficiary class that drives the load growth creating the need for interregional infrastructure in the first place.
- **The generalized layered cost recovery architecture extends this beneficiary-pays logic across multiple layers**. Both nuclear construction cost overruns and reliability vintage capacity payments are recovered through layered stacks where every constituency pays a defined share calibrated to its structural interest in the program’s success.
- **The cost overrun stack** runs: federal construction entity absorbs up to acquisition price; publicly-owned utility acquirer absorbs 100-120% of acquisition price through its rate base (addressing moral hazard through POU first-loss exposure); hyperscaler Reserve Fund contributions cover 120-150%; federal tail-band absorbs catastrophic overruns above 150%.
- **The reliability vintage capacity recovery stack** runs: TVA absorbs ~15% of carrying cost through its wholesale rate (reflecting TVA’s own resource adequacy benefit); destination IOU absorbs ~15% through state-regulated retail rate base (reflecting destination region’s resource adequacy benefit); hyperscaler Reserve Fund contributions cover ~50% (reflecting hyperscalers’ role as principal load-growth drivers); federal appropriations cover ~20% as a bounded tail commitment.
- **The Nuclear Construction Reserve Fund expands to a Federal Anchor Program Reserve Fund** administering both coverage layers under a unified contribution structure. Hyperscaler contribution rates land in the $18-27/MWh range — substantially lower than single-layer single-payer approaches because POU, destination IOU, and federal absorption layers cover meaningful shares of each stack.
- **The structural integrity of the public-preference principle is preserved across both stacks.** Federal supports flow only to public utility acquirers and to anchor program asset owners; IPP eligibility doesn’t reopen. The moral hazard concern about federal absorption without accountability is addressed through POU first-loss exposure on cost overruns. The interregional transmission infrastructure case is supported through hyperscaler beneficiary-pays funding rather than through federal appropriations vulnerable to budget cycle pressures.
- **The destination IOU absorption layer requires a specific state regulatory framing.** The reliability vintage doesn’t earn capacity accreditation under PJM, MISO, or other RTO market rules, which complicates standalone prudency review under conventional principles. The layer is approvable at the state level through a package-deal framing: the state PUC’s relevant review unit is the IOU’s federal anchor program participation as a whole, not the reliability vintage in isolation. The IOU’s participation delivers substantial state-level benefits — federally-supported clean firm capacity at acquisition-price wholesale cost, federally-financed transmission infrastructure, supplemental bilateral reliability capability, continued data center load growth support — that the state PUC reasonably finds worth the associated retail cost recovery for the IOU’s 15% absorption share.
- **Virginia presents the strongest case** because Virginia’s regulatory environment uniquely combines binding state RPS legislation (the Virginia Clean Economy Act), the most concentrated data center load growth in the world, and PJM membership with the RTO’s recently volatile capacity market. The federal anchor program directly addresses Virginia-specific structural pressures that existing market mechanisms aren’t resolving on their own. Prospective VCEA amendment to include nuclear in the carbon-free definition would strengthen the case further by adding direct VCEA compliance value to the federal program’s deliveries — though the package-deal framing under current VCEA is substantively defensible without amendment.
- **The Fund’s contribution rate is set uniformly across all participating hyperscalers within defined rate-setting intervals** (probably 5-year periods), eliminating the first-mover penalty that pure actuarial rate-setting would create. Without uniform rate-setting, sophisticated hyperscaler procurement teams would recognize that delay is rewarded by lower future rates, no hyperscaler would benefit from going first, and the program might never reach first financial close. Rate adjustments based on accumulated fleet experience occur at interval boundaries; PPAs signed within an interval lock in the then-prevailing rate. Phase 1 sequencing requires minimum subscription thresholds (two or three pilots committed before any individual pilot reaches financial close) to maintain Fund risk-pooling diversification.
- **Hyperscaler PPA allocation within destination IOU territories is the IOU’s responsibility under state PUC oversight**, not a federal procurement decision. The likely allocation mechanism in each destination state blends bilateral negotiation with existing major customers (dominant, given concentrated hyperscaler presence in each destination state), competitive RFP for residual capacity, and state economic development considerations influencing negotiation terms. Inter-state competition for hyperscaler investment dynamically supports the program’s broader political coalition and geographic expansion.

## The Starting Question: Sizing for Interregional Reliability

The federal anchor program’s transmission infrastructure has been treated in the main proposal and the right-sizing companion as primarily a delivery mechanism — moving contracted clean firm generation from TVA territory to data center load in destination IOU territories, sized to the contracted wholesale share with engineering headroom and upgrade pathway provisioning for future additions financed against future contracted demand.

This framing is structurally honest for what it covers, but it leaves an important question unaddressed. The main proposal cites Winter Storm Elliott as operational justification for new interregional transmission — the rolling blackouts TVA and Duke Energy Carolinas implemented during the storm’s peak demonstrated that the Southeast suffers from inadequate interregional transfer capacity during stress events. The classical case for interregional transmission rests on resource adequacy benefits through geographic diversity: when load peaks, weather stress, and generation outages don’t coincide across regions, interregional capacity allows utilities to lean on each other’s non-stressed conditions. But a line sized only to contracted demand plus engineering headroom doesn’t provide substantial bidirectional emergency exchange capability — the reliability benefit is bounded to what the contracted vintage’s physical capacity supports.

This creates a real tension between public power proponents (concerned about ratepayer protection) and interregional transmission proponents (concerned about meaningful new infrastructure at scale). The threading-the-needle question is whether there’s a structural approach that satisfies both constituencies on the merits — building meaningful reliability infrastructure without loading the carrying cost onto current ratepayers through cross-subsidy. Answering it requires honest accounting of what the anchor lines actually buy for reliability and how the financing architecture should match cost incidence to benefit incidence.

## What the Anchor Lines Actually Buy for Reliability

The honest accounting starts with recognizing that the federal anchor plant produces some fixed nameplate output; the line moves that output between TVA territory and the destination region but doesn’t generate power itself. During grid stress events, the line’s bidirectional dispatch capability allows TVA to reclaim contracted flow for in-territory use, or to import emergency capacity from the destination RTO — but these flows reallocate existing capacity rather than producing new capacity. If TVA needs 300 MW during stress, it can cut delivery to the destination IOU by 300 MW or import 300 MW from the destination RTO, but either way it’s reallocating system capacity, not adding to total.

The reliability story therefore has three components, only one of which is transmission-specific. First and largest: in-territory generation. TVA acquires the new plants and operates them in its territory; the in-territory share (perhaps 1,000-1,200 MW per twin-AP1000) directly improves TVA’s resource adequacy. This is a generation benefit that exists with or without anchor transmission. Second: resource adequacy in the destination region from contracted wholesale flow. The destination RTO gets more firm clean capacity to serve growing data center load. Also primarily a generation benefit, with the line as delivery mechanism. Third and transmission-specific: bidirectional emergency exchange capability. During TVA stress, the line can reverse direction and import; during destination region stress, the line continues delivering (and potentially more than contracted share if TVA has surplus).

The bidirectional capability is bounded by what’s available on the other side of the seam. TVA importing from PJM only helps if PJM has surplus; if both regions are stressed, the line doesn’t help. The most useful capability is during asymmetric stress events. Winter Storm Elliott was actually symmetric across the broader Eastern Interconnection, where interregional transmission provides much less benefit than the classical case suggests — there’s no surplus on either side to send when everyone is stressed at once. Asymmetric events do happen and the line provides real value during them, but they’re a smaller share of the total stress landscape than the classical case implies.

The honest reliability sizing for the anchor lines therefore lands in the 300-500 MW range of bidirectional emergency exchange capability per line — meaningful value during asymmetric stress without overstating what the line can do during symmetric stress. Building this capacity into the lines requires sizing them above contracted demand by 300-500 MW per line, a defined reliability vintage on top of the contracted vintage.

## The Cost Recovery Problem

Sizing the lines with a reliability vintage on top of the contracted vintage creates an immediate cost recovery problem. Each segment owner builds infrastructure sized for the full physical capacity (contracted plus reliability vintage), but only the contracted vintage produces wholesale rate or retail PPA revenue. The reliability vintage’s carrying cost has to be recovered from somewhere.

Take the Clinch River → Northern Virginia line at 2,000 MW total (1,500 MW contracted Dominion vintage + 500 MW reliability vintage). TVA owns and rate-bases the Tennessee HVDC portion and sending converter station at 2,000 MW. The federal anchor authority owns the cross-jurisdictional segment and 65% of the receiving converter station, also sized at 2,000 MW. Dominion owns the downstream AC leg to the Loudoun County data center cluster, sized to deliver 2,000 MW, plus 35% economic stake in the receiving converter station. Under straightforward rate-basing, TVA’s wholesale rate to Dominion has to recover the full 2,000 MW capital cost even though only 1,500 MW of contracted flow generates wholesale revenue. Dominion’s retail PPA recovers the full 2,000 MW on Dominion’s segment even though only 1,500 MW generates retail revenue. The 500 MW reliability vintage produces no contracted commercial revenue, and without an alternative funding source its carrying cost would flow to contracted vintage customers — exactly the cross-subsidy problem the right-sized approach was designed to avoid.

Three structural approaches to fixing the cost recovery problem are available. First: federal appropriations cover the full reliability vintage carrying cost across all segment owners. The federal authority pays TVA and the destination IOU annualized capacity charges for reserving 500 MW on their respective segments for emergency exchange, calibrated to cost-of-service recovery. Every dollar of reliability vintage capital is financed federally; no cross-subsidy. Second: size the line to contracted vintage only, with bidirectional dispatch operating within that envelope rather than as additional physical capacity. The emergency exchange capability exists but is bounded by contracted vintage capacity and constrained by curtailment provisions; the cost recovery problem disappears because there’s no additional infrastructure to cover. Third: federal ownership of parallel reliability vintage infrastructure across all segments — structurally clean but practically difficult because federal ownership within TVA’s home jurisdiction conflicts with TVA’s statutory authority, and federal ownership of AC infrastructure within destination state IOU territories requires substantial federal preemption.

Approach one is the structurally honest answer if the federal commitment to interregional infrastructure is genuine and sustainable. Approach two is honest if the federal commitment is more constrained. Approach three is structurally interesting but practically difficult. The choice between them is fundamentally about how comprehensive the federal infrastructure commitment is.

## Federal Appropriations as an Interregional Capacity Market

Approach one is structurally a federally-administered, federally-funded, single-buyer capacity reservation arrangement: the federal authority pays TVA, the destination IOU, and itself (for the federal middle segment) annualized capacity charges for reserving the reliability vintage. This is a tightly bounded capacity market — federally-set prices, federally-defined service, long-term reservation agreements rather than auction-cleared dynamics. The closest existing analog is BPA’s transmission service tariff structure. The federal authority is effectively creating a market that doesn’t otherwise exist because no U.S. RTO currently allows transmission infrastructure to earn capacity credit (confirmed by MISO’s December 2025 HVDC Whitepaper).

Cost-of-service pricing — annualized capacity charges calibrated to actual segment capital plus defined returns — is the most administratively conservative methodology. Under cost-of-service across the four Phase 1 pilots, the aggregate annualized federal commitment is approximately $300-450 million per year (with $2.5-3.5 billion in construction-capital appropriation), or $15-20 billion cumulatively across a 40-year operating life. This is real money representing a substantial political ask through every appropriations cycle. The federal commitment is correspondingly larger and harder to defend than the original proposal’s federal-cost-overrun-tail-band-only commitment, with political defensibility depending on Congress sustaining appropriations across decades — a risk that has historically been hard to manage even for well-justified federal infrastructure programs.

## The Hyperscaler-Funded Alternative

The structural alternative to federal appropriations is to apply the beneficiary-pays logic that already underlies the Nuclear Construction Reserve Fund to the reliability vintage capacity payments. Hyperscalers drive the load growth creating the need for new interregional infrastructure; hyperscaler operations directly benefit from grid resilience during stress events; hyperscaler PPA cost structures can absorb additional contribution rates within reasonable parameters. The Nuclear Construction Reserve Fund framework expands to a Federal Anchor Program Reserve Fund with two coverage layers — the original nuclear cost-overrun absorption plus reliability vintage capacity payments to anchor line asset owners — funded by hyperscaler per-MWh contributions through the same mechanical structure.

This is structurally cleaner than pure federal appropriations because it ties reliability vintage funding to the beneficiary class that drives the underlying need for the infrastructure. The federal commitment shrinks to a bounded backstop rather than carrying the full federal infrastructure burden directly. But the expansion is also more demanding on hyperscalers — under pure hyperscaler funding the total contribution rate runs in the $40-55/MWh range, with realistic negotiation likely landing closer to $35-50/MWh and federal appropriations covering a residual gap of perhaps $100-150 million per year. And it asks one constituency (hyperscalers) to absorb the majority of reliability vintage costs while other constituencies that benefit from the program contribute nothing additional, which is its own kind of asymmetry.

## The Generalized Layered Stack

The hyperscaler-funded alternative improves on pure federal appropriations but still asks one constituency to bear cost that others benefit from. The structurally cleaner approach is to layer the cost recovery across multiple parties with each paying a defined share calibrated to its share of benefit. The same logic applies to the nuclear cost overrun absorption that the original proposal addressed through the Nuclear Construction Reserve Fund — the original three-layer structure (federal up to acquisition price, hyperscaler 100-120%, federal tail above 120%) doesn’t include any first-loss exposure for the publicly-owned utility acquirer, which creates the moral hazard concerns opponents have correctly identified.

The generalized framing is that both cost overruns and reliability vintage capacity payments should be recovered through layered stacks where every constituency with a structural interest pays a defined share. The layering matches cost incidence to benefit incidence, which is the structurally honest answer to who should pay for what.

### The Cost Overrun Stack

The layered cost overrun stack runs:

**Federal construction entity** absorbs up to acquisition price. This is unchanged from the original proposal: the federal balance sheet bears construction risk up to the agreed acquisition price set at financial close, with the plant transferred to the publicly-owned utility acquirer at this price regardless of actual construction cost.

**Publicly-owned utility acquirer** absorbs 100% to 120% of acquisition price. The POU’s first-loss exposure addresses the moral hazard concern that federal absorption without operator accountability creates cost-discipline problems. The POU absorbs the modal overrun outcome (most modern AP1000 deployments have come in at 110-130% of original estimate), creating genuine financial incentive to manage construction costs aggressively even though the federal government is the construction principal. For a $16 billion twin-AP1000, this layer covers $0-3.2 billion of overruns above acquisition price. The POU recovers its absorbed share through its rate-base mechanisms — TVA through its wholesale rate over the plant’s operating life, NYPA through state-regulated rates, and so on.

**Hyperscaler Reserve Fund** absorbs 120% to 150% of acquisition price. The Fund’s hyperscaler band covers overruns beyond the POU’s first-loss exposure up to a defined threshold. For the $16 billion twin-AP1000 example, this layer covers $3.2-8.0 billion of overruns. The Fund’s resources come from hyperscaler per-MWh contributions on offtake from federally-anchored plants, with the contribution rate calibrated to expected overrun magnitudes across the fleet under the layered structure.

**Federal tail band** absorbs above 150% of acquisition price. Catastrophic overruns above the Fund’s coverage threshold flow to the federal balance sheet through Treasury borrowing or Congressional appropriations. For the $16 billion twin-AP1000, this layer engages only at overrun magnitudes above $8 billion — truly extreme scenarios that represent tail risk no specific beneficiary can reasonably absorb.

The layered cost overrun stack addresses three structural concerns that the original proposal handled less directly. It addresses moral hazard through POU first-loss exposure (the POU has skin in the game on cost discipline). It preserves federal commitment to plant completion (the Fund and federal tail band still cover catastrophic overruns so the VC Summer scenario of stranded PPA commitments is prevented). It bounds the federal direct-absorption exposure to truly catastrophic overrun magnitudes that represent genuine tail risk.

### The Reliability Vintage Capacity Recovery Stack

The analogous stack for reliability vintage capacity payments runs:

**Publicly-owned utility acquirer (TVA on a Phase 1 pilot)** absorbs approximately 15% of reliability vintage carrying cost through its wholesale rate, reflecting TVA’s own resource adequacy benefit from the bidirectional emergency exchange capability. The infrastructure improves TVA’s reliability through emergency import capability and reduces TVA’s peak load risk through interregional exchange. For a typical HVDC pilot’s 500 MW reliability vintage at $200-300 million annualized total cost, TVA absorbs roughly $30-45 million per year. This cost is recovered through TVA’s wholesale rate to all customers in the TVA system (LPCs, directly-served industrial customers), reflecting the broad TVA-wide resource adequacy benefit.

**Destination IOU (Dominion on the Clinch River pilot)** absorbs approximately 15% of reliability vintage carrying cost through state-regulated retail rate base, reflecting the destination region’s resource adequacy benefit. For the Clinch River pilot, Dominion absorbs roughly $30-45 million per year. The cost is recovered through Dominion’s general retail rates (not specifically through the hyperscaler PPA), reflecting that the benefit accrues to general retail customers’ resource adequacy across Northern Virginia and the broader Dominion service area, not solely to hyperscaler load.

**Hyperscaler Reserve Fund** absorbs approximately 50% of reliability vintage carrying cost through expanded Fund contributions, reflecting hyperscalers’ role as principal load-growth drivers. For the Clinch River pilot, the Fund covers roughly $100-150 million per year of reliability vintage cost. The Fund’s resources come from the same hyperscaler per-MWh contributions that fund the cost-overrun layer, with contribution rates calibrated to cover both layers.

**Federal appropriations** absorb approximately 20% of reliability vintage carrying cost as a bounded tail commitment, reflecting the program’s broader national infrastructure value beyond what any specific beneficiary captures. For the Clinch River pilot, federal appropriations cover roughly $40-60 million per year. Across the four Phase 1 pilots, the federal appropriation requirement is approximately $150-250 million per year — substantially smaller than the $300-450 million per year required under pure federal appropriations and politically more defensible because it represents the residual that no specific beneficiary funds alone.

The reliability vintage capacity recovery stack addresses the cost recovery problem that pure ratepayer cross-subsidy or pure federal appropriations approaches couldn’t solve cleanly. Every party with a structural interest in the reliability vintage’s existence pays a defined share of its carrying cost. No party is asked to bear the full cost; no party is fully insulated from the cost.

### Why the Layering Is Structurally Better

The single-payer approaches we considered along the way had political and structural problems because they tried to load the full reliability vintage cost onto one constituency that wasn’t the sole beneficiary. The layering approach matches cost incidence to benefit incidence, which is the structurally honest answer to who should pay for what.

The TVA absorption layer is defensible because TVA does benefit from the reliability vintage. The infrastructure improves TVA’s own resource adequacy through emergency import capability during asymmetric stress events affecting TVA but not the destination region. TVA absorbing 15% of reliability vintage carrying cost reflects TVA’s actual share of benefit, recoverable through TVA’s wholesale rate to all customers in the TVA system. The TVA CEO’s commitment that data center growth won’t pressure rates for other electric customers is preserved because the reliability vintage benefits all TVA customers, not just data center load.

The destination IOU absorption layer is defensible on the substantive merits — Dominion, Duke, Entergy MS, and Southern all benefit from the reliability vintage’s contribution to their respective regions’ resource adequacy — but it raises a specific state regulatory question that warrants careful treatment. The reliability vintage doesn’t earn capacity accreditation under PJM, MISO, or other RTO market rules, so standalone rate base prudency analysis under conventional principles is more complicated than for typical transmission investments. The destination IOU absorption layer is approvable at the state level through the package-deal framing developed in the State PUC Defensibility section below, which reframes the relevant prudency review unit from the reliability vintage in isolation to the IOU’s federal anchor program participation as a whole.

The hyperscaler Fund contribution layer is defensible because hyperscalers are the principal drivers of the load growth that creates the need for interregional infrastructure at this scale, and because the reliability vintage directly supports hyperscaler operations during stress events. The hyperscaler Fund contribution is the largest single layer in the stack, reflecting hyperscalers’ largest share of structural responsibility for the program’s existence.

The federal appropriations layer is defensible because the program also delivers broader national infrastructure value beyond what any specific beneficiary captures — interregional grid resilience, AP1000 industrial-base support, U.S. competitive positioning in clean firm generation deployment. The federal layer is the smallest of the four, but it represents the federal commitment to broader public goods that no single beneficiary funds alone.

## State PUC Defensibility: The Package-Deal Framing

The destination IOU absorption layer requires state PUC approval, and the substantive grounds for that approval deserve specific attention because the reliability vintage doesn’t fit neatly into standard rate base prudency analysis.

The challenge is structural. The reliability vintage capacity doesn’t earn capacity accreditation under PJM, MISO South, or other RTO market mechanisms. MISO’s December 2025 HVDC Whitepaper confirms that no U.S. RTO currently allows transmission infrastructure to earn capacity credit through standard accreditation rules. The federal anchor program’s reliability vintage therefore doesn’t reduce the destination IOU’s RTO capacity procurement obligation and doesn’t appear as a quantifiable resource adequacy contribution under existing market rule structures.

State PUCs reviewing IOU rate base under standard prudency principles typically require demonstrable value to ratepayers that justifies their bearing the cost through rates. If the reliability vintage’s value isn’t captured through RTO market accreditation, conventional prudency analysis runs into the question: what concrete benefit are retail customers receiving in exchange for paying these rates?

### Reframing the Prudency Review Unit

The package-deal framing answers this question by reframing the state PUC’s review unit. The PUC isn’t approving the reliability vintage as standalone infrastructure that has to clear prudency review independently. The PUC is approving the IOU’s participation in the federal anchor program as a whole, with the reliability vintage as one component of overall program participation that delivers substantial state-level benefits.

The benefit package for each destination state is substantial: access to federally-supported clean firm capacity at acquisition-price wholesale cost (substantially below equivalent IOU-built nuclear); federally-financed transmission infrastructure that wouldn’t otherwise be built; supplemental bilateral reliability capability through emergency exchange with TVA (real operational value during asymmetric stress events even without RTO accreditation); and continued infrastructure investment supporting load growth. The state PUC’s prudency analysis under this framing weighs these benefits against the IOU’s general retail customer cost recovery for reliability vintage carrying cost (approximately $30-45 million per year per destination IOU, or roughly $1-3 per retail customer per year). The benefit package is substantial enough that the reliability vintage cost is reasonably approvable as a component of overall program participation, even without standalone prudency justification through RTO market accreditation.

The critical structural feature is that program participation is a package, not a menu. The state PUC can’t approve some elements (federal clean firm delivery, federal transmission investment, data center growth support) without also approving the reliability vintage cost recovery that’s an integral component of IOU participation. The PUC’s binary choice is whether the IOU participates at all — if it doesn’t, the destination state forgoes all program benefits, which the PUC would not view as a favorable outcome. This is how state regulators actually approve novel infrastructure participation in practice: as a whole rather than as standalone analysis of each component.

### Virginia as the Strongest Case

Virginia presents the strongest substantive case for state PUC approval under the package-deal framing because Virginia’s regulatory environment creates Virginia-specific structural pressures that the federal anchor program is uniquely positioned to address.

Virginia’s electricity market is unusual in combining (a) binding state RPS legislation through the Virginia Clean Economy Act, (b) the most concentrated data center load growth in the world in Northern Virginia, and (c) PJM membership with PJM’s volatile market-cleared resource adequacy framework. This combination creates structural pressure that the federal anchor program directly addresses but that PJM’s existing market mechanisms aren’t solving on their own.

The Virginia Clean Economy Act mandates carbon-free electricity for Dominion by 2045 with intermediate procurement obligations. The VCEA as written excludes nuclear from the carbon-free definition, which creates an awkward compliance situation: nuclear is the most reliable clean firm capacity available, but Virginia’s RPS framework doesn’t count it. Dominion has to procure VCEA-compliant resources (solar, wind, storage) to meet RPS targets while also procuring firm capacity to meet PJM RPM obligations and to actually serve growing data center load reliably.

PJM’s capacity market has been highly volatile recently. The 2026/27 and 2027/28 Base Residual Auction results cleared at the FERC capacity cap ($329.17 and $333.44 per MW-day respectively), reflecting tight resource adequacy conditions across PJM. The 2024/25 BRA had cleared at $28.92 per MW-day just two years earlier; the swing has been extraordinary. Dominion’s capacity procurement costs have become substantially more volatile and substantially higher in recent years, creating real pressure on retail rates.

Northern Virginia data center load growth is the most concentrated in the world. Loudoun County alone hosts roughly 25% of global data center capacity by some measures, with continued growth projected to add 5-10 GW of additional load over the next decade. Dominion’s load forecast has been revised upward repeatedly, and the company’s existing generation portfolio and PJM capacity procurement strategy aren’t adequate to meet the projected growth at reasonable cost.

These conditions together produce a Virginia-specific structural argument for the federal anchor program that’s especially strong. Virginia faces unique pressure from the combination of VCEA’s nuclear exclusion, PJM’s volatile capacity pricing, and Northern Virginia’s extreme data center concentration. The federal anchor program addresses this pressure directly by providing federally-financed clean firm capacity (substantively addressing the resource adequacy challenge that PJM’s market is struggling with), delivered through federally-supported transmission (reducing dependence on PJM’s volatile capacity market), with bilateral emergency exchange capability (supplementing market-cleared resource adequacy with operational reliability infrastructure).

The VSCC’s prudency analysis under these conditions has a strong substantive foundation that doesn’t depend on PJM capacity accreditation. The federal anchor program addresses Virginia-specific resource adequacy and decarbonization pressures that PJM’s market mechanisms aren’t solving on their own. Dominion’s participation in the program is a prudent response to Virginia-specific conditions, and the rate-basing of Dominion’s reliability vintage share is a reasonable cost component of that prudent participation.

### Prospective VCEA Amendment Strengthens the Case Further

The case for Virginia program participation becomes substantially stronger if the Virginia Clean Economy Act is amended to include nuclear in the carbon-free definition. The amendment has been seriously discussed in the Virginia General Assembly in recent sessions, with Dominion lobbying for it and some environmental advocates softening as the federal program’s clean firm focus has clarified what nuclear can contribute.

Under a VCEA-includes-nuclear scenario, the federal anchor program’s deliveries to Virginia gain direct compliance value beyond their physical energy and resource adequacy contributions. Each MWh of federal anchor delivery to Dominion would generate VCEA-compliant attributes, reducing Dominion’s alternative procurement obligation under the RPS framework. The economic value of program participation increases substantively because federally-supported clean firm capacity that’s also RPS-compliant is more valuable than federally-supported clean firm capacity that doesn’t count toward state compliance targets.

The improvement flows to multiple constituencies. Dominion’s overall VCEA compliance cost decreases because federally-supported nuclear is meaningfully cheaper than alternative VCEA-compliant procurement (utility-scale solar with storage, offshore wind). Virginia retail customers benefit from the lower all-in compliance cost flowing through to retail rates over time. Northern Virginia hyperscalers benefit from access to VCEA-compliant clean firm capacity through Dominion retail PPAs, supporting their corporate clean energy commitments while meeting their substantial firm capacity needs.

This creates positive feedback dynamics for the program’s political economy in Virginia. The federal program strengthens the case for VCEA amendment (because the practical existence of federally-supported nuclear serving Virginia data center load makes the VCEA’s nuclear exclusion increasingly artificial), and VCEA amendment strengthens the case for federal program participation (because participation now delivers direct compliance value alongside resource adequacy and economic development benefits).

The federal program’s authorizing legislation doesn’t require VCEA amendment as a condition of Virginia participation. The package-deal framing under current VCEA is substantively defensible on its own. But the federal program should explicitly contemplate the favorable interaction with state-level RPS reform, both to support eventual VCEA amendment in Virginia and to position the program favorably in other destination states that may pursue similar RPS framework adjustments.

### Generalization to Other Destination States

The Virginia argument is the strongest, but versions apply to the other Phase 1 destination states with calibrated adjustments. North Carolina has emerging data center growth around Charlotte and the Research Triangle, the HB 951 carbon plan framework (softened by SB 266 in 2025 but still operative), and Duke Energy Carolinas membership in SERC rather than an organized RTO; the substantive case is somewhat weaker than Virginia’s because data center concentration is smaller and the RPS framework is less binding, but the package-deal logic still works. Georgia has substantial data center growth around Atlanta and a continuing economic development orientation; Georgia Power’s continued participation in Vogtle 3 & 4 cost recovery demonstrates the Georgia PSC has accepted substantial retail customer cost burdens for nuclear infrastructure, and the federal anchor program offers a substantively similar pattern at lower retail customer cost. Mississippi has emerging central Mississippi data center growth and Entergy Mississippi’s MISO South membership, with a regulatory environment generally favorable to utility participation in regional infrastructure programs.

In each destination state, the substantive case rests on the package-deal framing rather than on standalone reliability vintage prudency analysis. Virginia presents the strongest case; the other states are calibrated versions of similar logic with somewhat less acute structural conditions.

### Why This Pathway Beats State Legislative Authorization

An alternative approach would be state-level enabling legislation explicitly authorizing destination IOU participation in the federal program with associated rate recovery. State enabling legislation would resolve the regulatory question at the legislative level, providing more durable authorization. But this pathway requires parallel legislative action in four destination states with each state’s timeline, political dynamics, and substantive language needing to align with the federal program’s design — substantial political coordination requirements that make program enactment contingent on multiple state legislative outcomes the federal program proponents have limited control over.

The package-deal regulatory pathway works within existing state regulatory discretion. State PUCs already have authority to approve utility participation in federal infrastructure programs under package-deal logic; the federal anchor program doesn’t require new state authorization to invoke this authority. The political coordination requirements are limited to standard state regulatory engagement during PUC proceedings. State legislative authorization can be pursued where strategically valuable (Virginia’s VCEA amendment being the most promising case), but the federal program’s overall participation framework doesn’t depend on it.

## The Federal Anchor Program Reserve Fund

The Nuclear Construction Reserve Fund described in the main proposal expands to a Federal Anchor Program Reserve Fund administering both coverage layers under a unified contribution structure. The Fund’s expanded design becomes more complex but more structurally complete.

### Contribution Structure

Hyperscaler per-MWh contributions on offtake from federally-anchored plants fund both the cost-overrun coverage layer and the reliability vintage capacity payment layer. The contribution rate is calibrated to cover both layers under the layered stack structure and is set uniformly across all participating hyperscalers within defined rate-setting intervals (see Uniform Rate-Setting and the First-Mover Problem below). Aggregating the two layers, hyperscaler total Fund contributions land in the $18-27/MWh range:

- Cost overrun coverage (120-150% band): approximately $10-15/MWh contribution
- Reliability vintage capacity payments (50% layer): approximately $8-12/MWh contribution

This is substantially lower than the $40-55/MWh range under single-layer approaches because the POU, destination IOU, and federal absorption layers cover meaningful shares of both stacks. The lower hyperscaler contribution rate keeps hyperscaler PPA pricing competitive with alternative clean firm options, supports broader hyperscaler buy-in to the program, and provides headroom for future contribution rate adjustments if Fund accumulation proves inadequate during the operating period.

For a hyperscaler offtaking 1,000 MW at 90% capacity factor from a federally-built plant, the total Fund contribution is approximately $140-215 million per year per plant. Across the Phase 1 fleet of four plants, aggregate hyperscaler contributions to the Fund are roughly $560 million to $860 million per year.

Hyperscaler contributions begin during the construction period rather than waiting for commercial operation. PPA contracts include advance contribution provisions calibrated as a fraction of expected lifetime operational contributions, paid annually during construction with credits applied against operational contributions once delivery begins. Without these advance contributions, the Fund would have essentially no reserves when the first pilot reaches commercial operation, leaving the coverage band nominally available but functionally empty for the earliest pilots. The advance contribution rate is calibrated so that across the construction period (probably 6-10 years for AP1000 deployments), Fund reserves accumulate to roughly $2-4 billion by the time the first pilot reaches commercial operation — adequate for early-pilot coverage even if a Vogtle-scale overrun emerges on the first pilot.

The mechanism is structurally analogous to CWIP (Construction Work in Progress) financing in utility ratemaking, with three differences that distinguish it from traditional CWIP. Cost-bearers are the program’s direct beneficiaries (hyperscaler offtakers who will receive plant output) rather than the general ratepayer base. Funds flow into the federal trust fund as protected reserves rather than into general utility revenue. The commercial commitment is voluntary at the individual hyperscaler PPA level rather than imposed through rate-case proceedings. The cost discipline accountability that’s traditionally a concern with CWIP operates through other layers of the cost overrun stack — federal construction entity, POU first-loss absorption, Fund administration — rather than relying on the advance contribution mechanism itself. If a pilot is cancelled during construction, advance contributions made on that pilot remain in the Fund as part of the broader Phase 1 reserve pool rather than being refunded; this is consistent with the Fund’s risk-pooling logic and is a real but bounded commercial concession from hyperscalers that the program design should acknowledge.

### Disbursement Structure

The Fund’s disbursement structure handles two distinct payment streams.

Cost overrun disbursements flow to the federal construction entity to cover overruns in the 120-150% band on specific plants. Disbursements are triggered by documented overrun cost reporting and verification, with payments made as construction progresses through the overrun band. Plant-specific tracking ensures that Fund disbursements match actual overrun absorption requirements on specific projects.

Reliability vintage capacity payments flow to the asset owners (TVA, destination IOUs, federal anchor authority) for their middle-band share of reliability vintage carrying cost. Payments are made as annual capacity charges calibrated to cost-of-service recovery on the reliability vintage share of each owner’s infrastructure capital. The 50%-of-total-cost share that the Fund covers means that on a $300-450 million per year aggregate reliability vintage cost across the Phase 1 pilots, Fund disbursements for capacity payments are approximately $150-225 million per year.

Total annual Fund disbursements across both layers are approximately $200-400 million per year during normal operations (when cost overruns are within or below the Fund’s coverage band), rising during years when significant overruns occur on specific plants.

### Uniform Rate-Setting and the First-Mover Problem

The Fund’s contribution rate methodology has to balance two competing structural considerations. On the actuarial side, the rate should reflect expected risk across the participating fleet — early pilots face higher concentrated risk because Fund reserves are thin and operational experience is limited, while later pilots benefit from accumulated reserves and refined actuarial estimates. Pure actuarial rate-setting would therefore charge higher rates to early hyperscaler PPAs and lower rates to later ones as the Fund matures.

But pure actuarial rate-setting creates a coordination failure. If hyperscalers know that later PPAs will be cheaper, no individual hyperscaler benefits from going first. Every hyperscaler reasons that waiting is better; if every hyperscaler waits, the program never gets to first financial close, the Fund never accumulates reserves, and the dynamic that would justify the rate differential never starts. This is a classic first-mover penalty problem that recurs across infrastructure and risk-pooling programs, and it’s particularly acute for federal anchor program PPAs because hyperscaler procurement teams are sophisticated enough to recognize the dynamic and to delay commitment if delay is rewarded.

The structural fix is uniform fleet-wide rate-setting within defined intervals. All hyperscaler PPAs signed within a given interval pay the same Fund contribution rate, regardless of which pilot they’re contracting with or when within the interval they sign. The rate is set initially at program launch based on expected fleet-wide actuarial experience across the Phase 1 fleet, with rate adjustments occurring only at interval boundaries based on accumulated experience. Within each interval, no hyperscaler is penalized for going first relative to going later, eliminating the coordination failure that pure actuarial rate-setting would create.

The rate-setting interval is probably 5 years — long enough to provide meaningful PPA rate stability for hyperscaler procurement planning, short enough to allow rate adjustments as accumulated experience develops. Interval boundaries should be set at predictable dates rather than tied to specific pilot milestones, so hyperscalers can plan around them without strategic timing concerns. A hyperscaler signing a PPA in year 3 of a 5-year interval pays the same rate as a hyperscaler signing in year 1 of the same interval; both rates may be adjusted at the interval boundary based on accumulated experience.

Phase 2 expansion operates within the same rate-setting framework rather than triggering a separate Phase 2 Fund or separate Phase 2 rate calibration. Phase 2 pilots join the existing Fund under the existing rate-setting framework, with Phase 2 hyperscalers paying the same rate as Phase 1 hyperscalers within each interval. This preserves the program’s geographic scaling while preventing reintroduction of first-mover penalty dynamics at the Phase 1/Phase 2 transition.

The structural trade-off is that uniform rate-setting transfers some risk to the federal tail-band that would otherwise have been absorbed through differentiated early-pilot premiums. The federal tail-band exposure (catastrophic cost overruns above 150% of acquisition price) is bounded and is appropriately federal in any case, but the program design should explicitly acknowledge that uniform rate-setting modestly increases expected federal tail-band engagement compared to pure actuarial rate-setting. This is a reasonable trade-off because eliminating the first-mover penalty is essential to program viability — without uniform rate-setting, the Fund may simply not accumulate enough subscriptions to begin operating, in which case the entire program structure fails.

The credibility of the uniform rate-setting commitment matters. Hyperscaler procurement teams will probe for any opportunity to delay commitment if they perceive that delay is rewarded. The federal program’s authorizing legislation should specify the uniform rate-setting methodology with statutory definition of interval lengths, calibration procedures, and rate adjustment protocols. Statutory commitment provides the credible signal that ad hoc administrative practice could not, preventing strategic timing by hyperscaler offtakers within Phase 1 and across the Phase 1/Phase 2 transition.

### Administration and Governance

The Federal Anchor Program Reserve Fund is administered by Treasury or DOE as a dedicated federal trust fund, with defined statutory criteria and oversight requirements. The fund’s governance includes:

Federal oversight of contribution rate setting, with rates calibrated annually to actuarial assessments of expected overrun magnitudes across the fleet and reliability vintage carrying cost trajectories. The contribution rate methodology is published and subject to public comment, with adjustments occurring within defined parameters.

Federal oversight of disbursement criteria, with cost-overrun disbursements requiring documented construction cost reporting and reliability vintage capacity payments requiring verification of asset availability and operational performance. Disbursement decisions are subject to standard federal accountability mechanisms.

Hyperscaler input on Fund governance through a structured advisory mechanism. Hyperscalers as the principal contributors have legitimate interest in Fund administration; the advisory mechanism provides structured input without giving hyperscalers veto authority over Fund decisions.

State PUC engagement on the destination IOU absorption layer through normal state regulatory processes. IOU rate-base treatment of the destination IOU’s reliability vintage absorption share flows through state PUC approval as part of the IOU’s standard rate proceedings.

The administrative complexity is bounded and serves clear structural purposes. Each layer has a defined coverage scope, a defined funding source, and a defined disbursement mechanism. The Fund administration framework can handle this through standard trust-fund accounting techniques established in other federal trust-fund programs.

## Operational Mechanics: Sequencing and PPA Allocation

The Fund’s contribution structure, the uniform rate-setting methodology, and the layered cost recovery architecture together create the financial framework for the program. The operational mechanics — how Phase 1 pilots actually sequence to financial close, and how individual hyperscaler PPAs get allocated within destination IOU territories — build on this framework but require their own structural treatment.

### Phase 1 Sequencing and Minimum Subscription Requirements

The Fund’s risk-pooling integrity depends on portfolio diversification across multiple participating pilots. If only one pilot subscribes initially, the Fund’s exposure to that pilot’s overrun risk isn’t diversified; a Vogtle-style outcome would deplete the Fund before other pilots could contribute to it. The program design should therefore establish minimum subscription thresholds before any individual pilot reaches financial close.

A defensible minimum is two or three Phase 1 pilots subscribed before any single pilot can close. Subscription requires (a) the Phase 1 IOU partner has committed to wholesale offtake, (b) hyperscaler retail PPAs are signed with the destination IOU for the pilot’s contracted vintage under the uniform rate-setting framework, and (c) hyperscaler Fund contribution commitments are in place. The federal anchor authority’s administrative role includes verifying that subscription requirements are met before authorizing individual pilots to proceed to financial close.

Under this approach, Phase 1 pilots subscribe in parallel rather than sequentially. The first two or three pilots reach subscription approximately together, with PPA terms negotiated under the same Fund contribution rate (uniform within the interval) and financial close occurring once the minimum threshold is reached. Subsequent Phase 1 pilots subscribe as their site readiness and contractor availability allow, without waiting for earlier pilots to reach commercial operation.

The Fund begins accumulating contributions during construction. Hyperscalers committed to PPAs on subscribing pilots pay Fund contributions on their contracted offtake commitments before the plants reach commercial operation, with contributions calibrated to expected delivery volumes. The Fund accumulates reserves during the construction period (probably 6-10 years for AP1000 deployments), so by the time the first pilot reaches commercial operation the Fund has substantial reserves available for cost-overrun disbursement and reliability vintage capacity payments.

Phase 2 sequencing operates similarly. Phase 2 pilots subscribe as their public power acquirers (NYPA, NPPD, LADWP, others) and destination IOU partners are ready, without waiting for Phase 1 completion or Phase 1 commercial operation. The Phase 1 minimum subscription threshold doesn’t apply to Phase 2 because the Fund by then has accumulated reserves from Phase 1 subscriptions that provide the diversification benefit. Phase 2 pilots simply join the existing Fund framework at the prevailing rate within the relevant rate-setting interval.

### Hyperscaler PPA Allocation Within Destination IOU Territories

The contracted vintage capacity on each Phase 1 pilot (approximately 1,200-1,500 MW per pilot under right-sized scope) has to be allocated among hyperscaler offtakers competing for access. The destination IOU is the retail seller; the IOU’s allocation mechanism is subject to state PUC oversight. The federal program structure constrains the framework within which allocation occurs but leaves specific allocation decisions to the destination IOU under state regulatory oversight.

Three allocation mechanisms could be used, each with different implications. Open competitive RFP issues a request for proposals from qualified hyperscaler offtakers, with defined eligibility criteria and evaluation criteria, with the IOU awarding capacity to winning bidders. This is the most transparent mechanism and the easiest for state PUC oversight, but it doesn’t necessarily produce the strategically best allocation from the destination state’s perspective.

Bilateral negotiation with existing major customers leverages the IOU’s existing commercial relationships with hyperscalers operating in its service territory. Dominion negotiates with Northern Virginia hyperscalers, Southern with Metro Atlanta hyperscalers, Duke with Charlotte/RTP hyperscalers, Entergy Mississippi with Central Mississippi hyperscalers. This produces results that reflect existing market structure but may foreclose competitive entry.

State-coordinated allocation ties PPA access to broader economic development goals. Hyperscalers commit to specific investment, employment, and tax contribution levels in exchange for access to federal anchor program PPA capacity; the state coordinates the IOU’s allocation based on economic development value. This captures additional state-level value but adds political dimensions that complicate state PUC oversight.

In practice, the actual allocation in each destination state will probably blend elements of all three approaches, weighted toward bilateral negotiation for the bulk of the allocation but with competitive RFP for residual capacity and state economic development considerations influencing the negotiation terms. The structural reason is that the federal anchor program’s PPA allocation is happening in a market where major hyperscaler offtakers already have concentrated commercial relationships with destination IOUs. Northern Virginia data center load is dominated by a handful of hyperscaler companies with substantial Dominion infrastructure; the same companies are the natural offtake counterparties for federal anchor program deliveries to Dominion. Pure open RFP would produce results substantially similar to bilateral negotiation in most cases.

The likely Phase 1 allocation pattern, based on existing hyperscaler footprints in destination states, is roughly as follows. Northern Virginia (Dominion via Clinch River) has Amazon as the largest data center operator, with Microsoft, Google, Meta, and Oracle all maintaining substantial existing infrastructure; the 1,500 MW contracted vintage might be allocated across 3-5 major hyperscalers receiving 300-500 MW each. Metro Atlanta (Southern via Bellefonte) has Google, Microsoft, Meta, and other operators with meaningful presence; the 1,200 MW contracted vintage might be allocated across 2-4 hyperscalers. Charlotte and RTP (Duke via Hartsville) have Apple, Google, Meta, and Microsoft data center operations in the Carolinas; the 1,200 MW contracted vintage might be allocated across 2-4 hyperscalers. Central Mississippi (Entergy MS via Yellow Creek) has Meta (Forest, MS facility), Amazon (Madison County investment), and Microsoft (recent announcements); the 1,000 MW contracted vintage might be allocated across 2-3 hyperscalers.

The federal program should establish federal-level oversight of destination IOU allocation processes to ensure consistency with the program’s structural integrity, but should leave specific allocation mechanism to state PUC determination within each destination state. The federal oversight focus is on Fund contribution rate compliance and Fund administration integrity, not on dictating specific PPA terms. The destination IOU’s allocation under state PUC oversight is the appropriate locus for decisions about which hyperscalers receive federal anchor program PPA access.

Inter-state competition for hyperscaler investment will dynamically influence allocation outcomes. Once the federal program is operational, hyperscalers will make siting decisions partly based on which destination states offer federal anchor program PPA allocations. Destination states want to maximize their federal anchor program allocations and make their PPA terms attractive to hyperscalers considering siting decisions. This dynamic supports the program’s broader political coalition and geographic expansion: Phase 2 pilots become more attractive to potential public power acquirers when destination states want federal anchor program access.

The hyperscaler buy-in that the program depends on for the Fund’s contribution stream should be secured through industry-wide engagement during the federal legislative process, not through individual hyperscaler PPA negotiations. The structural premise — that hyperscalers as a class benefit from the federal anchor program and should contribute to Fund coverage of cost overruns and reliability vintage capacity payments — requires hyperscaler industry agreement on the broad contribution framework. Specific individual hyperscaler PPA terms then operate within the contribution framework, with the bilateral and competitive dynamics determining which specific hyperscalers participate in each pilot.

## Political Implications

The layered cost recovery approach changes the political dynamics around the program in meaningful ways. Each party with structural interest in the program faces a different ask than the original proposal contemplated, and each has a corresponding defensibility argument.

For the publicly-owned utility acquirer, the structure asks POU absorption of cost overruns in the 100-120% band and approximately 15% of reliability vintage carrying cost — meaningful first-loss exposure that addresses the moral hazard critique but requires institutional buy-in from TVA and Phase 2 candidates (NYPA, NPPD, LADWP). The defensibility is that this exposure is bounded and manageable through standard rate-base mechanisms, with the Fund and federal tail band covering catastrophic overrun scenarios that would otherwise destabilize the POU’s balance sheet.

For the destination IOU, the structure asks ~15% reliability vintage absorption through state-regulated rate base, recoverable from general retail customers under the package-deal framing developed earlier. The IOU’s other program benefits — transmission rate-base position, retail relationship with hyperscalers, access to federally-financed firm clean capacity — more than offset the additional reliability vintage absorption.

For hyperscalers, the structure produces a lower total Fund contribution rate than single-layer approaches ($18-27/MWh rather than $40-55/MWh) but covering both cost overrun and reliability vintage layers, with construction-period advance contributions providing bootstrap reserves. The lower rate keeps federally-anchored capacity competitive with alternative clean firm options while ensuring the federal infrastructure that serves hyperscaler load growth is durably financed.

For the federal government, the structure represents bounded but real ongoing commitment — federal tail band on cost overruns (above 150% of acquisition price) and approximately $150-250 million per year in reliability vintage appropriations across the four Phase 1 pilots. The federal role becomes catalyst and tail-risk absorber rather than principal funder, leveraging substantially larger contributions from POUs, IOUs, and hyperscalers and producing total program financing several times the federal share.

## Recommendations for Implementation

The layered cost recovery architecture should be incorporated into the federal anchor program’s authorizing legislation through specific provisions.

The cost overrun stack structure should be specified statutorily: federal balance sheet up to acquisition price, POU absorption in the 100-120% band, hyperscaler Fund coverage in the 120-150% band, federal tail band above 150%. The POU absorption layer requires the POU’s institutional buy-in but can be made operationally tractable through clear statutory definition.

The reliability vintage capacity recovery stack structure should be specified statutorily with target share percentages: approximately 15% POU absorption, approximately 15% destination IOU absorption, approximately 50% hyperscaler Fund coverage, approximately 20% federal appropriations. The exact percentages can be calibrated based on actual cost structures, but the layered framework should be statutorily defined.

The Federal Anchor Program Reserve Fund should be statutorily established with both coverage layers under unified administration. Treasury or DOE as administrator, with statutorily defined contribution rate methodology (uniform rate-setting within defined intervals, probably 5 years, plus construction-period advance contributions to address the bootstrap problem), disbursement criteria, and governance provisions. The Fund’s expanded scope from the original Nuclear Construction Reserve Fund framing requires explicit legislative authorization but otherwise builds on existing federal trust-fund administrative frameworks.

The destination IOU absorption layer requires state PUC engagement through standard regulatory proceedings rather than through state legislative authorization. The package-deal framing developed in the State PUC Defensibility section above provides the substantive basis for PUC approval: the IOU’s federal anchor program participation as a whole delivers state-level benefits substantial enough that the reliability vintage cost recovery is a reasonable component of overall participation. NARUC interlocution and structured state PUC consultation during the federal legislative process can preview the package-deal approach and identify state-specific implementation considerations. Virginia’s VSCC presents the strongest substantive case given Virginia-specific structural pressures around VCEA compliance, PJM capacity volatility, and Northern Virginia data center concentration; the other Phase 1 destination states (North Carolina, Georgia, Mississippi) present calibrated versions of similar logic.

The POU absorption layer requires the POU’s institutional commitment, which for TVA means TVA board engagement and any necessary TVA Act amendments to accommodate contingent capital absorption authority. For Phase 2 POUs (NYPA, NPPD, LADWP), the equivalent state-level institutional commitments are required.

The hyperscaler Fund contribution layer requires hyperscaler buy-in to the expanded contribution structure. Hyperscaler engagement during the federal legislative process should establish the framework as economically reasonable within hyperscaler PPA cost structures and operationally workable within Fund administration.

The federal appropriations layer requires annual Congressional appropriations for the federal tail share of both cost overruns and reliability vintage carrying cost. The appropriation magnitudes are bounded and structurally defensible as residual federal commitment, but they remain subject to standard appropriations cycle politics.

The program’s operational mechanics — Phase 1 sequencing with minimum subscription thresholds and hyperscaler PPA allocation within destination IOU territories — should be specified in the program’s administrative framework alongside the financial architecture. The Federal Anchor Authority verifies subscription requirements (probably two or three Phase 1 pilots committed) before authorizing individual pilots to proceed to financial close. Destination IOUs allocate contracted vintage capacity under their state PUC’s regulatory oversight, using whatever mix of bilateral negotiation, competitive RFP, and state economic development coordination is appropriate to each destination state’s regulatory environment. Federal oversight focuses on Fund contribution rate compliance and subscription threshold verification rather than on dictating specific PPA terms.

## Conclusion

The layered cost recovery architecture answers the interregional reliability question concretely. The anchor lines deliver real bidirectional emergency exchange capability — approximately 300-500 MW per line of asymmetric stress event reliability infrastructure — financed through a layered cost recovery architecture that preserves ratepayer protection on the contracted vintage and ensures the reliability vintage is paid for by the parties that benefit from it. Every party with structural interest in the program’s success pays a share calibrated to its share of benefit: POU first-loss absorption addresses moral hazard; destination IOU absorption reflects destination region benefit; hyperscaler Fund contributions reflect load-growth driver responsibility; federal appropriations cover bounded tail risk. No party bears full cost; no party is fully insulated from cost.

The Federal Anchor Program Reserve Fund as the unified administrative mechanism brings administrative coherence to the program’s financing architecture. Hyperscaler contributions in the $18-27/MWh range cover the largest share of both layers while remaining economically reasonable within hyperscaler PPA cost structures, with construction-period advance contributions providing bootstrap reserves and uniform rate-setting within defined intervals eliminating first-mover penalty dynamics.

This is the cleanest version of the program’s financial architecture that has emerged across the proposal’s development. The architecture is more demanding than the original proposal’s framing but correspondingly more defensible across the program’s political and structural constituencies. Some version of this layered cost recovery framework is likely necessary for the federal anchor program to deliver its full potential — substantial new clean firm generation acquired by public utilities, meaningful new interregional transmission infrastructure with bidirectional reliability capability, and a durable financing architecture that survives political and budgetary pressures across decades of operating life.
