# How Interregional Transmission Advocates Think About HVDC “Reliability Headroom”

## TL;DR

- **No major interregional transmission advocate explicitly endorses building HVDC capacity that is deliberately withheld from energy or capacity markets as “dark” reliability-only headroom.** The advocate consensus — across Grid Strategies, ACEG, Brattle, ESIG, RMI, Niskanen, R Street, and Edison Electric Institute — is that *every* megawatt of interregional capacity, including uncommitted/non-firm capacity, should be *compensated* for the reliability value it provides, with intertie optimization (real-time energy markets) and ELCC-based capacity accreditation as the two preferred mechanisms.
- **However, the advocate community would functionally tolerate a “contracted-vintage-plus-reliability-vintage” architecture** *if* the reliability vintage receives explicit RA/ELCC capacity accreditation and compensation, even without continuous commercial energy flows. Brattle’s Pfeifenberger and the Brooks/Silverstein/Gramlich June 2025 report come closest to this position — but neither endorses leaving the reliability vintage *uncompensated*. The dominant critique throughout the literature is **economic underutilization** (capacity not earning revenue when it has value), not physical underutilization per se.
- **The Pacific Intertie is invoked inconsistently** — as a model of beneficial seasonal/surplus interregional exchange (RMI, ACEG) and as a cautionary tale of underutilization (E3, NARUC, LBNL). The contemporary commercialization push (Western EIM, intertie optimization, CAISO Subscriber-PTO) is broadly framed as *correcting* the Pacific Intertie’s legacy underutilization, not as replicating it. For a federal anchor proposal contemplating a substantial reliability vintage, the political and analytical sweet spot is structuring the reliability vintage as ELCC-accredited capacity with bidirectional EIM/SEEM participation enabled — not as appropriated, market-isolated reserve.

## Key Findings

### 1. The dominant frame is “compensate, don’t isolate”

The unifying frame across the advocate corpus is that interregional transmission today receives **inadequate compensation for the reliability value it provides** — not that lines should be deliberately built with reserve capacity outside markets. Johannes Pfeifenberger (Brattle) put this most directly in his April 2025 “Intertie Optimization” deck: “This resource adequacy and resilience value of even ‘uncommitted’ interregional transmission is recognized only by some RTOs/ISOs (e.g., NYISO and ISO-NE). Other RTOs (such as PJM) attribute very little resource adequacy value to existing interregional transmission and zero value to new interregional transmission. None of the RTOs compensate the resource adequacy and resilience value of uncommitted interregional transmission provided on a merchant basis.”  Pfeifenberger’s prescription is **commercialization** through intertie optimization plus RA accreditation, not deliberate reservation.

### 2. The Grid Strategies June 2025 RA report is silent on energy-market revenue — but its ELCC framework would accommodate “dark” reliability vintage

The Brooks/Silverstein/Gramlich June 2025 report “Resource Adequacy Value of Interregional Transmission” (Grid Strategies, ACEG, Grid United) is the most directly relevant document. It argues that interregional lines should receive RA value through one of two mechanisms: “reducing the LSE’s capacity obligation or accrediting and compensating the transmission line owner.”   Critically, the report explicitly contemplates RA value “even without firm generation associated with the facility.”  The ELCC tables in the report — for North Plains Connector, per the Astrapé Consulting NPC Evaluation Final Report (June 2024, reviewed by PNNL), show “1,800 MW in WECC, 1,350 MW in SPP, and 400 MW in MISO”  — summing to 3,550 MW of capacity value on a 3,000 MW nameplate (118.3%). These tables presume that *non-firm* scarcity-period flows generate the RA value. **The report does not affirmatively say “the reliability headroom need not earn energy market revenue.”** But its analytical framework is fully compatible with that outcome, provided the capacity is ELCC-accredited and compensated.

### 3. DOE’s Transmission Facilitation Program is a gap-filler, not a reliability reserve

Critical for the federal anchor proposal: DOE’s TFP capacity-contract awards (SWIP-N, Cross-Tie [now terminated by TransCanyon], Aroostook, Cimarron Link, Southline, Southern Spirit) are unambiguously designed as **temporary commercial gap-fillers that DOE intends to resell**, not as standing federal reliability reserves. DOE’s program FAQ states: “If the line is not fully subscribed by other customers by the time the line is finished and providing service, then DOE will begin making capacity contract payments. In that case, DOE will continue to seek a transmission service buyer to assume DOE’s capacity commitment and will also market its capacity on a short-term basis to recover its costs.”  Rob Gramlich praised this design feature in POWER Magazine: “that sequence — allowing for the DOE’s capacity reservation before transmission construction, and then allowing the line’s subscribers to pay back the government — is a notable step forward.”  A federal anchor proposal that contemplates *permanent* uncommercialized federal reliability vintage is **a structural departure from how the existing TFP works**, and advocates have not built rhetorical scaffolding for it.

### 4. The “underutilization” critique is overwhelmingly economic, not physical

When LBNL, E3, NARUC, Brattle, and Grid Strategies critique “underutilized” interregional transmission, they almost always mean **economic underutilization** — capacity unused when there is a positive price differential — not low average physical utilization. The 2026 LBNL paper by Safavi, Mulvaney Kemp, Gorman, Millstein, and Wiser quantifies the inefficiency: “26% of all net flow on the interfaces we study between 2014 and 2023 displaces relatively cheaper within-region generation with more expensive generation and… most interfaces are regularly underutilized, even when the energy being exchanged is economical.”  The framing is that low utilization is acceptable when prices don’t justify flow, but problematic when arbitrage opportunities are missed. The Brattle/Willkie Farr report — authored by Pfeifenberger, DeLosa III, and Gonzalez (Brattle) with former FERC Chair Norman C. Bay and Vivian W. Chum (Willkie Farr & Gallagher), sponsored by ACORE, Advanced Power Alliance, Grid United, Invenergy, MAREC Action, and NRDC  — estimates “$50–60 million annually in additional value for every gigawatt of intertie capacity beyond what bilateral trades are currently able to capture”  — value lost because operators don’t optimize. This frame strongly favors *some* mechanism for the reliability vintage to flow opportunistically when prices justify it.

### 5. Pacific Intertie cited inconsistently — both model and cautionary tale

RMI invokes the Pacific Intertie as a success story: “As far back as 1961, President John F. Kennedy directed an interregional transmission study that ultimately led to the construction of the Pacific Intertie. This vital link between California and the Pacific Northwest has consistently proven its value — for resilience, customer savings, and emissions reductions.”  The historical record bears out the “partly firm, partly surplus” structure: the Northwest Power and Conservation Council notes “Most of the power transmitted on the Intertie is surplus to regional needs, but some firm power also is transmitted. For example, Bonneville sells about 1,900 megawatts of firm energy to customers in California via the Intertie.”  But that same partly-non-firm structure is what E3 and NARUC critique in their “Unlocking Interregional Transmission” report: “Recent historical data for the operation of existing interregional transmission lines indicate that interregional interties are often underutilized even when flows are most valuable… [due to] economic charges to schedule and transmit power between regions; scheduling requirements up to 75 minutes before energy is delivered… and a lack of reliable protocols for how to operate interregional lines during times of extreme grid conditions.”  The contemporary push for Western EIM, CAISO WEIM, intertie optimization, and SEEM is largely framed as *correcting* the Pacific Intertie–era inefficiency.

### 6. Merchant HVDC subscription model is contested — most clean-energy advocates want more market participation, not less

The merchant HVDC subscription-anchor model (SOO Green, CHPE, Grain Belt Express, North Plains Connector) is treated by advocates as a *workable financing structure* that nevertheless has unfinished business at FERC and RTOs. CHPE’s NYISO market integration was approved by FERC on April 13, 2026 (docket ER26-570). Per Utility Dive: “The Federal Energy Regulatory Commission on Monday approved market rules for the Champlain Hudson Power Express project, a $6-billion, 1,250-MW merchant transmission line that is set to begin delivering hydropower from Canada to New York City in May.”  The market rules include full ISO scheduling control, LMP settlement at the Astoria Proxy Bus,  capacity market triggering provisions, and ICAP market parameters that treat the line’s capacity as ISO-dispatchable. SOO Green has pushed harder, arguing that PJM’s external capacity requirements should be relaxed for controllable HVDC lines: “Since the HVDC line is controllable, PJM can directly dispatch SOO Green to get the energy and capacity they need.”  NRDC, the Sustainable FERC Project, and the American Clean Power Association supported SOO Green’s complaint. The FERC October 2025 order (3-0) requiring MISO to specify how merchant HVDC is incorporated into transmission planning shows continued institutional movement toward integrating merchant lines into central market operations, not leaving them outside markets.

### 7. R Street and Niskanen explicitly want commercialization, not appropriations-funded reserve

Devin Hartman (R Street) frames reliability compensation as a market-discipline issue, opposing both incumbent-utility self-funding and appropriations-driven federal capacity. His 2023 RealClearEnergy piece argued for “monetizing the reliability benefit of firm power imports in competitive electricity markets”  and treating gas-pipeline-style voluntary contracting as the model. R Street’s November 2025 DOE RFI comments criticized DOE’s withdrawal of the Grain Belt Express loan precisely because it favored “routine and largely risk-free American Electric Power transmission system rebuild” over “competitive transmission projects using novel technology and voluntary cost allocation (i.e., market revenues, rather than captive customers).”  Liza Reed (Niskanen) testified to the Senate ENR Committee in March 2026 that “Grid operators do not compensate high-voltage direct-current transmission technology for some reliability services”  — framing the gap as missing compensation, not missing reserve.

### 8. Christina Hayes / ACEG and WIRES emphasize emergency value but don’t propose reserve-only structures

ACEG (Christina Hayes) consistently invokes Winter Storm Uri and Elliott to argue for interregional capacity that can “save lives when extreme weather hits,” and supports BIG WIRES Act-style minimum-transfer requirements. Larry Gasteiger (WIRES) similarly emphasizes resilience but does not articulate a model where reliability vintage is held outside markets. Both groups effectively endorse the “compensation through RA accreditation” pathway rather than appropriations-funded reserve.

### 9. The Niskanen “Unlocking HVDC” brief explicitly calls for compensating HVDC reliability services

The Niskanen Center brief by Robin Allen and Rachel Levine, “Unlocking HVDC: How Congress can enable a more resilient grid,” explicitly lists as a needed reform “Compensating HVDC operators for services that improve the reliability of the overall grid.”  This is the clearest advocacy statement that reliability headroom *should* receive compensation — but the brief frames it as a deficiency in current market structures (i.e., a problem to fix), not as an endorsement of leaving the capacity uncompensated.

### 10. No Princeton/MIT/LBNL paper models “dark headroom” as optimal

A targeted review of Princeton ZERO Lab (Jenkins/Ricks), MIT CEEPR, and LBNL EMP work found no paper that models deliberate reservation of HVDC capacity outside wholesale markets as an *optimal* design choice. The closest adjacent work (Manocha/Mantegna/Patankar/Jenkins on co-optimizing renewables inverter sizing) addresses generator interconnection capacity oversizing, not interregional reliability reserve. The MIT CEEPR analysis “Bridging the Gaps: The Impact of Interregional Transmission on Emissions and Reliability” by Botterud, Knittel, Parsons, Senga and Story (NBER Working Paper #32996) finds that “the BIG WIRES Act leads to lower system cost in the order of $487 million and $3.21 billion annually”  under current policies and 95% CO₂ reduction scenarios — but assumes the added transfer capability is used in security-constrained economic dispatch, not held in reserve. **There is a genuine literature gap here**: no surveyed advocate or academic source affirmatively endorses or models dark reliability headroom outside markets as optimal.

## Details

### What “headroom” means in the advocacy literature

The term “headroom” is rarely used in the advocacy literature for transmission capacity above firm contracted flows. The more common framings are:

- **“Uncommitted” capacity** (Pfeifenberger/Brattle): capacity not sold under firm transmission service agreements but physically available
- **“Non-firm” capacity** (BPA/WAPA legacy terminology, NW Power Council): capacity available subject to curtailment, typically priced as opportunity service
- **“Available transfer capability” (ATC)** (NERC, RTO tariff language): the standard engineering measure of unsubscribed capacity available for short-term commercial use
- **“Resource adequacy value of interregional transmission”** (Grid Strategies June 2025): the analytical framing that translates uncommitted capacity into ELCC-accredited capacity value

None of these terms map cleanly onto the “reliability vintage” concept in the federal anchor proposal as described. The advocate literature does not have an established term for “capacity deliberately built for emergency-only bidirectional flows and not commercialized in day-to-day markets.” This terminology gap is itself a finding: the federal anchor proposal is operating in a conceptual space the advocacy community has not yet built rhetorical infrastructure around.

### Does utilization rate matter as a metric?

Yes, but the framing matters. The LBNL 2026 paper, Brattle’s intertie optimization work, and Grid Strategies’ congestion reports all use *utilization rate during economic price spreads* as a key diagnostic — not raw average MW flow. The Brattle/Willkie Farr report estimates the “wrong-way flow” share at “approximately 40 percent of all real-time market intervals of the year”  for SPP/MISO/PJM interfaces, characterizing this as a market design failure rather than a transmission planning failure. The implication for the federal anchor proposal: a reliability vintage that flows at 5–10% physical utilization would not be criticized *if* every economic price spread justifying flow is captured; but if the vintage is institutionally walled off from real-time markets, the advocate community would press for it to be enrolled in EIM/SEEM/CTS-equivalent intra-hour optimization.

### Specific Brattle position on “even uncommitted” interregional capacity

Pfeifenberger’s NEMA presentation language — “Recognize the full resource adequacy value of interregional transfer capability (even if non-firm or not committed to capacity imports) to reflect load and resource diversity”  — is the strongest analytic endorsement of treating reliability-only headroom as a real, compensable asset. This is roughly the architecture the federal anchor proposal contemplates, *provided* the destination RTOs (PJM, MISO) actually credit the reliability vintage with capacity value. PJM today, per Pfeifenberger, “attribute[s] very little resource adequacy value to existing interregional transmission and zero value to new interregional transmission,” which would be a binding obstacle.

### NYISO/CHPE precedent for ISO-controlled HVDC market participation

The CHPE precedent (FERC approval of market rules April 13, 2026 in docket ER26-570; NYISO IRM/LCR/CAF modeling 2025) is the most fully developed example of an HVDC line whose capacity is **firmly contracted** (Hydro-Québec under 25-year NYSERDA contract for 10.4 TWh/year)  but whose **market participation is fully embedded in the ISO** — NYISO schedules transmission service,  settles energy at the Astoria proxy bus,  and treats the line’s capacity as ICAP “triggering” supply. There is no “dark” capacity on CHPE; even when not delivering, the line’s ratings and capacity contribution shape the ICAP market parameters.  This is the operating model the advocate community appears to expect for any new HVDC line — including ones with reliability vintage.

### SOO Green / Grain Belt FERC litigation: the merchant-line market access question

The pending FERC docket history — SOO Green’s two complaints against PJM (EL21-85), the October 2025 FERC order on MISO/Grain Belt planning, the 2023 R Street/Invenergy AD22-13 technical conference docket — all push in the direction of giving merchant HVDC *more* market access (capacity market participation, planning model inclusion), not less. The PJM Independent Market Monitor’s “subvert the PJM Capacity Market by permitting an inferior product”  line shows the institutional resistance, but advocate alignment is firmly with SOO Green’s position.

### Federal-financing precedents

Beyond DOE TFP, the historical federal financing precedents are WAPA’s PACI service (16 U.S.C. 837e provides “transmission capacity not required for the transmission of Federal energy shall be at equitable rates determined by the Secretary of Energy”)  and BPA’s Pacific Intertie service. Both treat federal capacity not used for federal power as **commercially sellable transmission service**, not as dark reliability reserve. WAPA’s 2025–2026 rate orders for PACI service explicitly provide formula rates for short-term sales (Rate Schedules PACI-C1, PACI-D1, PACI-W1).  The legal architecture for federally-financed transmission has always assumed surplus capacity will be marketed, not held in reserve.

## Recommendations

For a sophisticated policy analyst working on a federal anchor transmission financing proposal with explicit reliability vintage, here is what the advocacy landscape implies:

**Stage 1 — Frame the reliability vintage as ELCC-accredited capacity, not as appropriations-funded reserve.** The Brooks/Silverstein/Gramlich June 2025 report provides ready-made analytic scaffolding for crediting the reliability vintage with capacity value in both TVA’s resource adequacy footprint and the destination RTO. Drop “dark headroom” and adopt “ELCC-accredited reliability vintage” or “bidirectional resilience capacity” as the framing. This makes the proposal legible to Grid Strategies, ACEG, ESIG, Brattle, and Niskanen.

**Stage 2 — Make the reliability vintage market-eligible by default.** Even if the primary purpose is emergency bidirectional support, the line operator (federal or contracted) should be authorized to bid the reliability vintage into NYISO/PJM/MISO real-time energy markets, the SEEM 15-minute market, or future EIM/SAA constructs whenever stress conditions are not active. This pre-empts the “stranded capacity” critique that LBNL, Brattle, and E3 deploy against the Pacific Intertie–era status quo. The CAISO Subscriber-PTO model is the cleanest precedent — Pfeifenberger explicitly cites it as best practice.

**Stage 3 — Use the DOE TFP model as a structural template, not a literal copy.** DOE TFP capacity contracts are commercial-resale obligations. If the federal anchor proposal is to permanently retain reliability vintage as federal property, that is a structural departure from TFP. Either reframe the proposal so that the reliability vintage is **sold to TVA, the destination RTO’s transmission owners, or an interstate compact** with operational dispatch rights for stress events (a Western Interconnection–style RAS/RMR-equivalent arrangement); or be explicit that this is a *new* federal authority that goes beyond TFP, with appropriations as the funding source and an inter-RTO joint dispatch agreement defining stress-event activation.

**Stage 4 — Engage Brattle, Grid Strategies, and ESIG early for a “white paper” on reliability-vintage compensation models.** No such paper currently exists. The federal anchor proposal could fund or co-author one, filling the literature gap identified above. The natural authors are Pfeifenberger (Brattle), Brooks/Silverstein/Gramlich (Grid Strategies/ACEG), Allen/Reed (Niskanen), and Lasher (ESIG transmission resilience task force).

**Benchmarks that would change the staging:**

- If PJM or MISO files a tariff change accrediting non-firm interregional transmission with capacity value (mirroring NYISO/ISO-NE), the federal proposal can rely more on market compensation and less on appropriations.
- If FERC issues an order in the AD22-13 successor docket creating a generic framework for merchant HVDC capacity market participation, the proposal can pivot to a merchant-equivalent commercial structure.
- If NERC’s next ITCS update revises the November 2024 finding that “An additional 35 GW of transfer capability is recommended across the United States as a vehicle to strengthen energy adequacy under extreme conditions,”  the political case for appropriations-funded reliability vintage shifts accordingly — downward if the figure shrinks, upward if it grows.

## Caveats

1. **The advocate community has not directly engaged the “reliability vintage” concept as articulated in the proposal.** Inferring positions requires reading across the RA accreditation, intertie optimization, and merchant HVDC literatures. Direct quotes endorsing or rejecting uncommercialized reliability headroom do not exist in the surveyed corpus.
1. **The Brooks/Silverstein/Gramlich June 2025 report is silent on the revenue-stream question.** It is *compatible with* an ELCC-only revenue stream for the reliability vintage but does not affirmatively endorse it. The authors should be consulted directly.
1. **Pacific Intertie historical evidence is rhetorically two-edged.** Pro-transmission advocates invoke it as a success story; market-design reformers invoke its underutilization as a problem. The federal anchor proposal should be careful which framing it adopts and anticipate the opposing rhetorical move.
1. **Edison Electric Institute, Catalyst, Aurora, and Clean Energy States Alliance** were searched but produced limited explicit positions on this narrow question. EEI’s general posture favors incumbent-utility regulated transmission build with full cost recovery, which is structurally compatible with reliability vintage but politically distant from merchant-HVDC advocates.
1. **The PJM external-capacity rules remain a binding constraint.** Until the PJM IMM and PJM accept that controllable HVDC lines can deliver capacity to PJM without the line itself being directly dispatchable by PJM, any TVA-to-PJM reliability vintage will face the same institutional resistance SOO Green has faced. A federal proposal may need to preempt this through statutory direction to FERC.
1. **Inter-RTO joint dispatch over HVDC ties is an underdeveloped institutional concept.** MISO’s “Incorporating HVDC in Energy and Ancillary Services Market Operations” stakeholder work  is the closest live process. No advocate group has yet published a definitive proposal for cross-RTO joint dispatch of HVDC reliability vintage during stress events.
1. **The proposal’s “contracted vintage” of 1,000–1,200 MW nuclear baseload is aligned with how CHPE structures Hydro-Québec firm hydropower delivery**, and is uncontroversial in the advocate community. The novelty is entirely in the reliability vintage’s institutional treatment, where the literature is genuinely thin.
