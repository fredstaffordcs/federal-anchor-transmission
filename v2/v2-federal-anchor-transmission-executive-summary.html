Federal Anchor Transmission: Executive Summary

Federal Anchor Transmission: Executive Summary

Fred Stafford · fred@publicpowerreview.org Version 2.0 · May 20, 2026

Executive summary of "Federal Anchor Transmission: A Proposal for Interregional Buildout Through Federally-Built Clean Firm Generation."

The Idea

This proposal calls for federal construction of a portfolio of new clean firm generation plants on TVA’s pre-assessed nuclear sites -- Bellefonte in Alabama, Hartsville and Clinch River in Tennessee, Yellow Creek in Mississippi -- with each plant transferred to TVA at a defined acquisition price at commercial operation, and with associated interregional transmission built as jointly-owned segmented projects connecting TVA territory to the major data center load centers in neighboring IOU territories (Southern Company in Georgia, Duke Energy in the Carolinas, Dominion Energy in Northern Virginia, Entergy Mississippi in Central MS). The federal government bears construction risk during plant construction; TVA acquires plants at known, fixed prices regardless of actual construction cost; cost overruns above acquisition prices are absorbed through a layered architecture in which every constituency with structural interest pays a defined share calibrated to its benefit incidence.

The transmission template is the Pacific Intertie, the 1964 federal infrastructure project that connected Columbia River federal hydropower in the Pacific Northwest to load centers in California through a joint segmented architecture: BPA owned the northern segments, LADWP owned the southern segments, and California IOUs owned segments and converter stations in their territories. Each utility recovered its segment’s capital from its own customers; cost allocation was settled through ownership rather than contested allocation methodology. The corridor has operated reliably for sixty years and moves roughly 8,000 MW between regions. Its underlying logic -- anchor framing (a specific federal generation source giving the line a resource-to-load story rather than a generic capacity addition), segmented ownership (each utility owns its segment and recovers it from its own customers), and reciprocal regional benefit (each region’s generation backs up the other during off-peak periods) -- translates directly to the 21st-century clean firm generation challenge.

The Pacific Intertie’s segmented-ownership template plus the federal-balance-sheet construction model (the New Deal-era BoR/Corps/BPA framework) are generalized to the federal anchor transmission program. Each anchor line connects a federally-built clean firm plant in TVA territory to a destination IOU’s data center load. TVA owns the home-jurisdiction segment plus sending converter station; the Federal Anchor Authority owns the cross-jurisdictional segment plus 65% of the receiving converter station (with IOU minority economic stake of 35%, no operational control); the destination IOU owns the downstream AC infrastructure plus the 35% economic stake in the receiving converter station. TVA dispatches the federal segments under delegated operating authority, which severs commercial entanglement with the destination RTO’s market mechanics while preserving operational coordination.

The four Phase 1 pilots demonstrate the architecture across a deliberate range of structural contexts. Bellefonte → Metro Atlanta (~250 miles, 500kV AC, no RTO seam) is the simplest two-owner architecture: TVA-owned segment in Alabama, Southern Company-owned segment in Georgia, with handoff at the state line. Hartsville → Charlotte and Research Triangle (~350 miles, ±525kV HVDC, no RTO seam but cross-state) is the three-owner architecture: TVA segment, federal cross-jurisdictional segment, Duke AC leg. Clinch River → Northern Virginia (~450 miles, ±525kV HVDC, crosses TVA/PJM seam) uses HVDC specifically to sever PJM commercial entanglement at the seam -- PJM schedules AC integration at the receiving converter station but does not dispatch the HVDC link. Yellow Creek → Central Mississippi (~300 miles, ±525kV HVDC, crosses TVA/MISO South seam) demonstrates the cross-RTO architecture works at multiple seams. Each line carries contracted vintage (~1,000-1,500 MW per pilot) plus a defined reliability vintage of 300-500 MW for bidirectional emergency exchange capability during asymmetric stress events.

Hyperscaler offtake follows a wholesale-to-retail structure. TVA wholesales the contracted vintage to the destination IOU under a long-term capacity contract at acquisition-price-derived cost. The destination IOU sells retail to hyperscaler data center customers under PPAs that incorporate the wholesale cost plus IOU margin plus Federal Anchor Program Reserve Fund contributions. The retail customer relationship stays with the IOU; the IOU recovers transmission rate base on its segment; TVA recovers its acquisition cost through the wholesale rate. The structure preserves what each party values: the IOU keeps its retail customers, distribution rate-base, and long-term customer relationships; TVA gains scale expansion of its nuclear fleet without bearing construction risk; hyperscalers get firm clean capacity at predictable cost; the federal government catalyzes the buildout without becoming a retail electricity provider.

The Justification

After two decades of essentially flat U.S. electricity demand, load is growing again -- driven primarily by data centers and AI infrastructure, but also by electrification, manufacturing reshoring, and dispatchable thermal retirements. TVA reports data center load reached 18% of its industrial demand in 2025 and is projected to double by 2030. Dominion’s Northern Virginia data center load has driven its retail electricity sales growth from roughly 1% annually pre-2018 to 5-7% annually in the past three years, with PJM capacity prices in 2026/27 and 2027/28 BRAs clearing at the FERC-imposed cap of $329-333/MW-day. Duke faces analogous pressure in the Carolinas; Southern Company in Georgia is building generation as fast as Georgia Power’s regulatory environment allows; Entergy Mississippi’s Central MS data center cluster around Meta’s Forest facility and Amazon’s Madison County investment is straining the host utility’s resource adequacy planning. Most utilities cannot independently build enough generation to meet projected load growth on the timeline required.

Interregional transmission is the obvious solution. DOE’s National Transmission Needs Study, MIT’s The Future of the Electric Grid, and NREL’s interconnection studies converge on the conclusion that the U.S. needs roughly double its current interregional transfer capacity to meet 2040 demand reliably. NERC’s November 2024 Interregional Transfer Capability Study recommended an additional 35 GW of transfer capability across the United States as a vehicle to strengthen energy adequacy under extreme conditions. Beyond economic dispatch, interregional transmission delivers substantial resource adequacy benefits through geographic diversity: load peaks, weather stress, and generation forced outages don’t coincide across regions, so utilities connected by adequate interregional capacity need less redundant in-region capacity to meet reliability targets.

Winter Storm Elliott in December 2022 demonstrated the resource adequacy case operationally. Extreme cold across the Southeast caused widespread natural gas plant forced outages and fuel deliverability failures, and both TVA and Duke Energy Carolinas were forced to implement rolling blackouts on December 23-24, 2022 -- the first such events in either utility’s history. Limited interregional transfer capacity meant that the Southeast could not adequately draw on power from regions experiencing different conditions. The episode is honestly acknowledged as evidence for the reliability case while recognizing that Elliott was actually symmetric across the broader Eastern Interconnection (where interregional transmission provides less benefit than the classical asymmetric-stress case suggests). Asymmetric events do happen, and the reliability vintage provides real value during them, but the proposal sizes the reliability vintage at 300-500 MW per line -- meaningfully smaller than fleet-scale interregional transmission framing might support and meaningfully larger than contracted-demand-only sizing produces -- to deliver genuine asymmetric-stress-event value without overstating what the lines can do during symmetric stress.

The problem is that modern interregional transmission projects die at the cost-allocation stage. Grain Belt Express spent over a decade in regulatory and political conflict before reaching financial close on its first section. MISO MVP took years of contested allocation methodology debate. SOO Green has been in litigation with PJM since 2021 over capacity market eligibility for controllable HVDC. The federal toolkit has expanded substantially -- DOE’s Transmission Facilitation Program with capacity-contract awards for SWIP-N, Cimarron Link, Southern Spirit, and others; the Office of Energy Dominance Financing’s expanded "energy infrastructure" lending mandate; the October 2025 DOC/Westinghouse/Brookfield/Cameco $80 billion AP1000 partnership; the IRA Section 45U and 48E tax credits; FERC Order 1000 and its interregional planning processes -- but the cost-allocation barrier remains stubborn. The federal anchor program resolves it through anchor framing and segmented ownership: federal generation gives the line a specific resource-to-load story, and segmented ownership settles cost allocation through who owns what. This is the principal structural innovation that lets the program move forward where merchant-only and generic-interregional approaches have stalled.

The Principles

The proposal’s defining principle is a modernized public-preference principle. The original New Deal preference principle offered public power entities preferential access to federal hydropower at cost-of-service rates. The justification was that public utilities returned benefits directly to ratepayers and citizens, while IOU intermediation diverted some share of federal benefits to private shareholders. The principle structured federal power policy for nearly a century -- TVA, BPA, WAPA, SWPA, SEPA all operate under variants of it -- and has demonstrated long-term durability across multiple political eras.

This proposal generalizes the principle but updates the substance. The 21st-century barrier to clean firm generation buildout is not the price of power (hyperscalers willingly pay premium prices for clean firm capacity; PJM capacity prices have cleared at the FERC cap; nuclear PPA prices in the $90-130/MWh range have been signed). The 21st-century barriers are the construction risk of new nuclear (which has prevented new build at scale since Vogtle) and the cost-allocation barrier to new interregional transmission. The relevant federal supports are therefore construction-risk absorption and transmission financing/siting authority rather than wholesale price subsidies.

Only publicly-owned utilities can be acquiring entities. Investor-owned utilities can be transmission segment co-owners, wholesale buyers, and retail offtake intermediaries for hyperscaler customers, but cannot be acquirers of federally-supported clean firm plants and cannot directly receive federal construction-risk absorption. This preserves the structural separation between public and private utilities while defusing the historical objection to federal nuclear cost-overrun absorption -- that it would socialize private profit -- because the publicly-owned acquirer returns operating benefits to its ratepayers and citizens, not to shareholders.

The cost recovery architecture is layered across every constituency with structural interest in both nuclear construction cost overruns and reliability vintage carrying cost.

For nuclear construction cost overruns: the federal balance sheet covers 0-100% of acquisition price during construction (Treasury borrowing or appropriations); TVA absorbs 100-120% as first-loss exposure through rate base (this is the moral hazard defense -- pure federal absorption would create an incentive structure where the operating utility has limited financial accountability for construction performance, and the 100-120% layer ensures TVA bears genuine cost discipline pressure); hyperscaler Reserve Fund contributions cover 120-150%; federal tail absorbs above 150%. Each layer is bounded; no party bears full cost or is fully insulated from cost. For a $16 billion reference Bellefonte twin-AP1000 acquisition, the modal expected outcome has TVA absorbing $0-3.2 billion, the Fund absorbing $4.8 billion in the catastrophic-but-not-worst-case range, and the federal tail absorbing the residual above $24 billion only in true Vogtle-scale scenarios.

For reliability vintage carrying cost: TVA absorbs ~15% through wholesale rate (reflecting TVA’s resource adequacy benefit from the reliability vintage); destination IOU absorbs ~15% through state-regulated retail rate base (reflecting destination region benefit); hyperscaler Fund covers ~50% (reflecting hyperscalers’ role as principal load-growth drivers driving the need for new capacity in the first place); federal appropriations cover ~20% (reflecting bounded national infrastructure value). For the four Phase 1 pilots, aggregate annualized reliability vintage carrying cost is approximately $300-450 million per year, split across these layers: TVA absorption $45-67M/year, destination IOU absorption $45-67M/year, hyperscaler Fund contributions $150-225M/year, federal appropriations $60-90M/year.

The reliability vintage is structurally walled off from commercial dispatch markets. The 300-500 MW per line of bidirectional emergency exchange capability serves resource adequacy during asymmetric stress events but is reserved by federal statute for emergency exchange use only, with no eligibility for participation in RTOs, EIMs (including SEEM expansion), bilateral commercial dispatch arrangements, or any future commercial market mechanism that might be developed. The wall-off design is structurally demanding but necessary to preserve TVA’s market independence over time -- EIM participation creates regular operational data flows, real-time price signals influencing dispatch, and ongoing operational integration that erode market independence even without formal RTO membership. The historical analog is BPA’s continuous pressure to integrate into CAISO’s Western EIM, which BPA has resisted only through strong institutional commitment rather than through any structural feature preventing pressure from accumulating. The layered cost recovery architecture is itself the strongest political defense of the wall-off design: hyperscalers explicitly paying for the reliability vintage as infrastructure they benefit from forecloses the "wasted infrastructure" framing that commercialization advocates would otherwise deploy.

The Political Strategy

The proposal will face substantial organized opposition. Five distinct opposition vectors require management.

Adjacent IOUs cast as offtakers rather than generators (Southern, Duke, Dominion, Entergy MS) gain real benefits -- transmission rate base, preserved retail relationships, firm clean capacity at federally-financed cost without taking construction risk -- but lose the lead generation builder role they currently value. Southern’s Vogtle 3 & 4 experience established it as the lead operating partner for AP1000 deployment in the Southeast; the federal anchor program transfers that role to TVA. Dominion has aggressively pursued its own SMR development and positioned itself as the lead utility for Northern Virginia data center supply; the proposal makes Dominion the retail intermediary for federally-supported, TVA-operated nuclear, which is harder to defend in Richmond than the current "Dominion builds and serves" narrative. Duke occupies a middle position with SMR exploration interests but less in-house nuclear capital commitment. The likely posture is conditional opposition: support if terms are sufficiently generous, fight to maximize favorable terms during legislative drafting.

Non-adjacent IOUs (Ameren, Evergy, FirstEnergy, NextEra Energy, Xcel) get nothing under the proposal and will see it as creating competitive asymmetries that favor adjacent IOUs in data center siting decisions. Their opposition is unified through the Edison Electric Institute, which has historically been highly effective at killing federal energy policy proposals threatening member interests. EEI’s member utilities collectively serve essentially all U.S. retail electricity customers outside public power territories and contribute substantially to political campaigns at federal and state levels.

Independent power producers, especially Constellation Energy, face structural threat to their hyperscaler PPA commercial model. Constellation operates 21 reactors (~22 GW), the largest U.S. nuclear fleet, and has built substantial recent business around hyperscaler PPAs including the Three Mile Island Unit 1 restart for Microsoft. Federally-financed AP1000s at acquisition-price wholesale rates set a benchmark for nuclear PPA pricing that Constellation’s existing fleet must compete against. The IPP opposition has more credibility on technical grounds than EEI opposition because IPPs are themselves nuclear operators making the case for federal supports -- but acceding to IPP demands for cost-overrun absorption would fundamentally compromise the public-preference principle.

State Public Utility Commissions in skeptical jurisdictions may resist IOU participation on principle even where the host IOU wants to participate. Texas’s ERCOT-isolated grid and free-market regulatory tradition mean any federal anchor program proposal involving Texas IOUs faces active state-level opposition. South Carolina, after VC Summer, has institutional sensitivity to large nuclear projects. PUC opposition operates at the state level rather than blocking federal enactment, but can effectively prevent IOU participation by denying rate-base treatment or CPCN approvals.

Interregional transmission advocates (Grid Strategies, ACEG, Brattle, ESIG, Niskanen, RMI) present a different kind of opposition. Their consensus position is that uncommitted interregional capacity should be compensated through ELCC accreditation and intertie optimization (real-time market participation) -- which directly contradicts the program’s wall-off design choice. Pfeifenberger (Brattle) put this most directly in his April 2025 NEMA presentation: "This resource adequacy and resilience value of even ‘uncommitted’ interregional transmission is recognized only by some RTOs/ISOs (e.g., NYISO and ISO-NE). Other RTOs (such as PJM) attribute very little resource adequacy value to existing interregional transmission and zero value to new interregional transmission." The Brooks/Silverstein/Gramlich June 2025 Grid Strategies report "Resource Adequacy Value of Interregional Transmission" provides the most developed analytical framework. The advocates support the program’s underlying purpose (new interregional capacity addressing resource adequacy) but disagree with the design choice on commercialization.

The principal mitigation for adjacent IOU opposition is the IOU minority co-ownership option, which permits adjacent IOUs to take 30-40% economic stakes in plants alongside TVA as majority acquirer. TVA remains operator and lead acquirer; the IOU’s minority position provides rate-base ownership in generation without operational responsibility. This gives Southern, Duke, and Dominion something substantial: a rate-base position in the new federal nuclear fleet at substantially lower risk than independent IOU-built nuclear would entail, because the IOU’s exposure is bounded by the layered cost recovery architecture. The co-ownership option converts adjacent IOUs from conditional opponents to aligned supporters -- the single largest political mitigation in the program design.

Non-adjacent IOU opposition is addressed through Phase 2 geographic expansion (NYPA in New York, NPPD in Nebraska, LADWP in California, SRP in Arizona, CPS Energy, JEA) reducing the share of IOUs that get nothing, plus tiered IOU eligibility for transmission rate-base position even without acquirer status. IPP opposition is managed without structural concessions through sequenced engagement, operating partnership opportunities (Constellation could potentially operate plants under contract for TVA), and supply chain participation -- concession bandwidth is reserved for IOU minority co-ownership where structural integrity can be partially preserved. State PUC opposition is managed through formal consultation requirements, NARUC interlocution, and optional participation at the state level.

The interregional transmission advocate community requires substantive engagement on the merits. Mitigation has three components. Substantive: pursue ELCC accreditation for the reliability vintage where compatible with the wall-off design, drawing on the Brooks/Silverstein/Gramlich report’s analytical framework (which is compatible with ELCC accreditation that doesn’t require continuous commercial energy flows -- accreditation reflecting the reliability vintage’s resource adequacy contribution during stress events). Rhetorical: adopt advocate community language -- "ELCC-accredited reliability vintage," "bidirectional resilience capacity" -- instead of "dark headroom" or "uncommercialized reserve." The conceptual content is the same; the framing makes the proposal legible to the advocate community. Structural: engage Brattle, Grid Strategies, and ESIG early for a collaborative white paper filling the literature gap on reliability-vintage compensation models. No such paper currently exists, and the federal anchor proposal could fund or co-author one -- giving the advocate community structural ownership of the analytical infrastructure for the program.

The commercialization-as-wedge critique is a sophisticated structural attack distinct from the broader advocate community preferences. It depends on the "wasted infrastructure" framing of the reliability vintage and pushes for EIM participation as a stepping stone toward market integration. Statutory protections (reservation for emergency exchange use only, Federal Anchor Authority dispatch control, prohibition on commercial dispatch market participation) are necessary but not sufficient. Sustaining the program’s structural integrity will require ongoing political commitment from public power constituencies and from federal program leadership across multiple administrations -- the same dynamic BPA has navigated for decades with Western EIM pressure.

The counter-coalition is meaningful in scale: public power trade associations (APPA, NRECA), hyperscaler companies (Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta, Oracle, all of whom need firm clean capacity at predictable cost), the nuclear manufacturing supply chain (Westinghouse and its many subcontractors gaining a major source of AP1000 orders), national security and AI competitiveness advocates, climate and decarbonization advocates, and the states benefiting directly from Phase 1 pilots (Tennessee, Alabama, Mississippi, Virginia, North Carolina, Georgia, and adjacent regions through TVA’s broader footprint). The combined geographic distribution of program benefits provides a credible base of Congressional support.

Operations and Administration

The Federal Anchor Program Reserve Fund is the central administrative mechanism, administered by Treasury or DOE as a dedicated federal trust fund covering both cost recovery layers under unified administration. Hyperscaler per-MWh contributions on offtake from federally-anchored plants fund the program: approximately $10-15/MWh for the cost overrun coverage layer plus $8-12/MWh for the reliability vintage capacity payment layer, aggregating to $18-27/MWh total. This is substantially lower than $40-55/MWh under single-layer single-payer approaches because TVA, destination IOU, and federal absorption layers cover meaningful shares of both stacks. For a hyperscaler offtaking 1,000 MW at 90% capacity factor, the total Fund contribution is approximately $140-215 million per year per plant. Across the Phase 1 fleet of four plants, aggregate hyperscaler contributions are $560-860 million per year.

Two design features address structural problems that would otherwise prevent first financial close. Uniform rate-setting within defined intervals (probably 5-year periods, with rate adjustments at interval boundaries based on accumulated fleet experience) eliminates the first-mover penalty. Without uniform rate-setting, sophisticated hyperscaler procurement teams would recognize that delay is rewarded by lower future rates because early pilots face higher concentrated risk than later ones, no individual hyperscaler would benefit from going first, and the program might never reach first financial close. Uniform rate-setting eliminates this coordination failure by ensuring all PPAs signed within an interval pay the same rate regardless of which pilot they’re contracting with or when within the interval they sign. The structural trade-off is that uniform rate-setting transfers some risk to the federal tail-band; this is an acceptable trade-off because eliminating the first-mover penalty is essential to program viability.

Construction-period advance contributions structurally analogous to CWIP solve the bootstrap problem. Hyperscaler contributions begin during the construction period rather than waiting for commercial operation. PPA contracts include advance contribution provisions calibrated as a fraction of expected lifetime operational contributions, paid annually during construction with credits applied against operational contributions once delivery begins. Without these advance contributions, the Fund would have essentially no reserves when the first pilot reaches commercial operation, leaving the coverage band nominally available but functionally empty. The advance contribution rate is calibrated so that across the construction period (6-10 years for AP1000 deployments), Fund reserves accumulate to roughly $2-4 billion by the time the first pilot reaches commercial operation -- adequate for early-pilot coverage even if a Vogtle-scale overrun emerges. The mechanism differs from traditional CWIP in three structurally important ways: cost-bearers are the program’s direct beneficiaries (hyperscaler offtakers) rather than the general ratepayer base; funds flow into the federal trust fund as protected reserves rather than into general utility revenue; and the commercial commitment is voluntary at the individual hyperscaler PPA level rather than imposed through rate-case proceedings.

Phase 1 pilots subscribe in parallel with minimum subscription thresholds (probably two or three pilots committed before any individual pilot reaches financial close) maintaining Fund risk-pooling diversification. The Federal Anchor Authority verifies subscription requirements before authorizing individual pilots to proceed to financial close. Subscription requires (a) IOU partner commitment to wholesale offtake under a long-term capacity contract, (b) hyperscaler retail PPAs signed under uniform rate-setting, and (c) hyperscaler Fund contribution commitments in place. Phase 2 pilots subscribe as their public power acquirers and destination IOU partners are ready, without waiting for Phase 1 completion.

Hyperscaler PPA allocation within destination IOU territories is the IOU’s responsibility under state PUC oversight, not a federal procurement decision. The likely allocation mechanism in each destination state blends bilateral negotiation with existing major customers (dominant, given concentrated hyperscaler presence in each destination state), competitive RFP for residual capacity, and state economic development considerations influencing negotiation terms. Federal oversight focuses on Fund contribution rate compliance and subscription threshold verification rather than dictating specific PPA terms.

State PUC approval for the destination IOU absorption layer of reliability vintage carrying cost (~$30-45 million per year per destination IOU, or roughly $1-3 per retail customer per year) operates through a package-deal framing: the PUC isn’t approving the reliability vintage as standalone infrastructure requiring independent prudency review but is approving the IOU’s participation in the federal anchor program as a whole, with substantial state-level benefits bundled with the reliability vintage absorption. The benefits include federally-supported clean firm capacity at acquisition-price wholesale cost (substantially below equivalent IOU-built nuclear), federally-financed transmission infrastructure that wouldn’t otherwise be built, supplemental bilateral reliability capability through emergency exchange with TVA, and continued infrastructure investment supporting load growth. The critical structural feature is that program participation is a package, not a menu: the PUC’s binary choice is whether the IOU participates at all -- if it doesn’t, the destination state forgoes all program benefits. Virginia presents the strongest substantive case given Virginia-specific structural pressures around VCEA compliance, PJM capacity volatility, and Northern Virginia data center concentration.

Legislative requirements include the Federal Anchor Generation Authority (federal construction-phase ownership with defined transfer mechanisms, public-utility-only acquirer eligibility, acquisition price methodology), the Federal Anchor Program Reserve Fund (statutory trust fund with uniform rate-setting and advance contribution provisions, governance structure, inflow/outflow rules), federal cost-overrun absorption authority (statutory authorization for federal absorption above 150%, with defined limits and reporting requirements), TVA Act amendments (POU first-loss authority for the 100-120% absorption layer; ~15% absorption authority for reliability vintage; fence carve-out authorizing TVA to sell power from federally-acquired anchor program plants on a wholesale basis to neighboring IOUs; debt cap expansion from $30 billion to $50-60 billion to accommodate plant acquisition financing plus ongoing TVA capital needs), federal transmission siting authority (renewed federal backstop siting authority for anchor lines tied to federally-supported generation), and statutory protections for reliability vintage (explicit reservation for emergency exchange use only, prohibition on commercial dispatch market participation, Federal Anchor Authority and TVA dispatch control specifications). Most other instruments -- the DOC/Westinghouse partnership, Office of Energy Dominance Financing, IRA tax credits, FERC Order 679 transmission incentives -- are already in place under existing authority.

A federal Anchor Program Office administers program criteria, evaluates candidate projects, oversees construction-phase activities, manages plant transfers, administers the Fund, and coordinates federal support packages. Interagency coordination among DOE, DOC, FERC, TVA, NRC, and Treasury aligns program criteria, construction oversight, transfer mechanisms, fund administration, and rate-setting protocols. Federal exposure is bounded across the program: on the cost overrun stack, limited to the 0-100% construction phase plus the catastrophic tail above 150%; on the reliability vintage stack, approximately $60-90 million per year across the four Phase 1 pilots; the program’s principal funding burden falls on the hyperscaler beneficiary class through structured Fund contributions rather than on federal appropriations. The federal role becomes catalyst and tail-risk absorber rather than principal funder -- a structurally different model than the New Deal-era federal power programs in which the federal government was the principal funder, but one that fits the 21st-century context of hyperscaler-driven load growth and corporate willingness to pay for firm clean capacity.
