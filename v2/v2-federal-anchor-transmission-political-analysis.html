Federal Anchor Transmission: Political Opposition and Risk Mitigation

Companion document to “Federal Anchor Transmission: A Proposal for Interregional Buildout Through Federally-Built Clean Firm Generation.”

Fred Stafford · fred@publicpowerreview.org Version 2.0 · May 20, 2026

This companion document develops the political opposition analysis and mitigation strategies that accompany the main proposal. The main document presents the structural architecture of the Federal Anchor Transmission Program; this document analyzes the organized political opposition the proposal would face and outlines specific structural concessions that could reduce that opposition.

Version 2.0 adds substantial new analysis on two opposition vectors that Version 1.0 didn’t adequately address. First, the interregional transmission advocate community (Grid Strategies, ACEG, Brattle, ESIG, Niskanen, RMI, and others) has specific positions on how uncommitted interregional capacity should be compensated, and the federal anchor program’s reliability vintage design conflicts with the advocate community’s consensus position. Second, the reliability vintage’s existence creates a sophisticated commercialization-as-wedge critique that opponents could mount to push the program toward market integration over time. Both vectors require explicit treatment in the proposal’s political strategy.

Political Opposition: Who Will Fight This and Why

A proposal of this scope and structural ambition will attract substantial organized opposition. Honestly identifying the opposition matters for two reasons: it allows the proposal’s drafters to anticipate and address specific objections rather than discovering them mid-legislation, and it helps build the coalition needed to counter them. The principal opposition comes from five distinct groups, each with different motivations, different political reach, and different points of leverage.

Adjacent IOUs That Are Cast as Offtakers Rather Than Generators

The Phase 1 IOU partners — Southern Company, Duke Energy, Dominion Energy, and Entergy Mississippi — gain real benefits under the proposal (transmission rate base, preserved retail relationships, firm clean capacity at federally-financed cost without construction risk), but they also lose something they currently value: the ability to be the dominant generation builder in their service territories. Southern Company’s Vogtle 3 & 4 experience, however financially painful, established Southern as the lead operating partner for AP1000 deployment in the Southeast. The federal anchor program effectively transfers that role to TVA, demoting Southern to wholesale buyer and transmission partner. For an IOU that has spent decades building its institutional identity around being the dominant generation builder in its territory, that demotion is structurally threatening regardless of the financial benefits.

Dominion Energy presents the sharpest version of this conflict. Dominion has aggressively pursued its own SMR development and has positioned itself as the lead utility for Northern Virginia data center supply. The anchor program would route a significant share of that supply through TVA-acquired generation rather than Dominion-owned generation. Dominion’s regulatory and political posture in Virginia is built around being the comprehensive solution to its customers’ needs; a structure that makes Dominion the retail intermediary for federally-supported, TVA-operated nuclear is harder to defend in Richmond than the current “Dominion builds and serves” narrative.

Duke Energy occupies a middle position. Duke has SMR exploration interests but has not made the same magnitude of capital commitments to in-house nuclear development as Southern or Dominion. Duke may be the most receptive of the adjacent IOUs to a program that delivers firm clean capacity without requiring Duke to bear construction risk, but Duke will still resist any structural feature that meaningfully shifts its long-term capital deployment trajectory away from its own balance sheet.

The likely posture of these IOUs is conditional opposition: they will support the program if the terms are sufficiently generous on the transmission rate-base, retail margin, and wholesale price dimensions, but they will fight the program at the legislative and regulatory drafting stages to maximize those terms and minimize structural features that make TVA the long-term lead. Their lobbying will focus on issues like: limiting the duration of TVA’s wholesale capacity commitments; expanding the IOU’s role in plant operation; preserving optionality for the IOU to eventually take ownership of the plant after the federal acquisition price is recovered; and ensuring that hyperscaler contributions to the Reserve Fund don’t preferentially flow back to non-IOU offtakers.

IOUs in Regions Without TVA Adjacency

The geographically more dangerous opposition comes from IOUs that get nothing under the proposal because they are not adjacent to TVA or another qualifying public utility, and whose competitive position would be eroded by IOUs in adjacent regions getting federally-supported firm clean capacity at favorable cost. Consider an IOU in the Midwest — say, Ameren or Evergy — that competes for data center siting decisions against IOUs in adjacent regions that get anchor program access. If Southern Company, Duke, Dominion, and Entergy Mississippi can offer hyperscalers retail PPAs backed by federally-financed nuclear at acquisition-price wholesale costs, while Ameren can only offer in-house generation built on its own balance sheet, Ameren is competitively disadvantaged.

The same dynamic applies to FirstEnergy in Ohio, NextEra Energy in Florida and the Gulf Coast, Xcel Energy in the upper Midwest, and others. Their opposition will be unified through the Edison Electric Institute (EEI), the principal trade association representing investor-owned utilities. EEI is a serious political force whose member utilities collectively serve essentially all U.S. retail electricity customers outside public power territories, contribute substantially to political campaigns, and has historically been highly effective at killing federal energy policy proposals that threaten member interests.

Independent Power Producers, Especially Nuclear Operators

The third major opposition force is the independent power producer (IPP) industry, with Constellation Energy as the most prominent and most directly threatened example. Constellation operates the largest U.S. nuclear fleet (21 reactors, ~22 GW) and has built substantial recent business around hyperscaler PPAs (the Three Mile Island Unit 1 restart for Microsoft, parallel deals with other hyperscalers).

The federal anchor program directly threatens Constellation’s commercial model. Federally-financed AP1000s at acquisition-price wholesale rates will set a benchmark for nuclear PPA pricing that Constellation’s existing fleet must compete against. Federally-built new nuclear competes directly with Constellation’s potential new-build pipeline. Hyperscaler contributions to the Reserve Fund effectively channel hyperscaler nuclear spending toward federal program plants and away from Constellation-built plants.

Constellation will oppose the program through direct Congressional lobbying, through the Nuclear Energy Institute (NEI), and through state-level utility regulators where Constellation has substantial operations. Vistra, Talen, NextEra Energy Resources, and other IPPs share Constellation’s structural concerns to varying degrees. The IPP opposition has more credibility on technical and policy grounds than the EEI opposition because the IPPs are themselves nuclear operators making the case that they should be eligible for federal supports — but the “expand eligibility to IPPs” argument fundamentally contradicts the public-preference principle the program is built on.

State Public Utility Commissions, Especially in Skeptical States

A fourth source of opposition is state public utility commissions (PUCs) in states where commissioners are skeptical of federal involvement in state-regulated retail markets. Texas’s ERCOT-isolated grid and strong free-market regulatory tradition mean any federal anchor program proposal involving Texas IOUs would face active state-level opposition independent of the IOUs’ own posture. South Carolina, after the VC Summer experience, has institutional sensitivity to large nuclear projects. Tennessee itself — despite hosting TVA — has elements of state government that resist federal expansion of any kind.

PUC opposition operates differently from IOU opposition because PUCs cannot directly block federal legislation, but they can block IOU participation in the program at the state regulatory level by denying rate-base treatment, denying CPCN approvals for anchor lines, or imposing unworkable conditions on IOU participation.

Interregional Transmission Advocates and the Commercialization Tension

Version 2.0 identifies a fifth opposition vector that Version 1.0 didn’t address: the interregional transmission advocate community, whose consensus position on uncommitted interregional capacity conflicts with the federal anchor program’s reliability vintage design choice.

The advocate community — Grid Strategies (Rob Gramlich and colleagues), ACEG (Christina Hayes and the Americans for a Clean Energy Grid coalition), Brattle Group (Johannes Pfeifenberger), ESIG (Energy Systems Integration Group), Niskanen Center (Robin Allen, Rachel Levine), RMI, R Street, and elements of EEI — has spent years building rhetorical and analytical infrastructure around the proposition that interregional transmission is undervalued by U.S. market structures. The unifying frame is that interregional capacity should be compensated for the reliability value it provides, with intertie optimization (real-time energy market participation) and ELCC-based capacity accreditation as the two preferred mechanisms.

Pfeifenberger’s April 2025 “Intertie Optimization” presentation put this most directly: “This resource adequacy and resilience value of even ‘uncommitted’ interregional transmission is recognized only by some RTOs/ISOs (e.g., NYISO and ISO-NE). Other RTOs (such as PJM) attribute very little resource adequacy value to existing interregional transmission and zero value to new interregional transmission. None of the RTOs compensate the resource adequacy and resilience value of uncommitted interregional transmission provided on a merchant basis.” Pfeifenberger’s prescription is commercialization through intertie optimization plus RA accreditation, not deliberate reservation.

The Brooks/Silverstein/Gramlich June 2025 Grid Strategies report “Resource Adequacy Value of Interregional Transmission” (co-published with ACEG and Grid United) is the most directly relevant document. It argues that interregional lines should receive RA value through “reducing the LSE’s capacity obligation or accrediting and compensating the transmission line owner,” with RA value possible “even without firm generation associated with the facility.” The ELCC tables in the report — for the North Plains Connector project, per the Astrapé Consulting evaluation reviewed by PNNL — show 3,550 MW of capacity value on a 3,000 MW nameplate (118.3%). These tables presume that non-firm scarcity-period flows generate RA value. The report does not affirmatively endorse leaving the reliability vintage uncompensated, but its analytical framework is compatible with that outcome provided the capacity is ELCC-accredited and compensated.

The Niskanen Center brief by Robin Allen and Rachel Levine (“Unlocking HVDC: How Congress can enable a more resilient grid”) explicitly calls for “compensating HVDC operators for services that improve the reliability of the overall grid” — the clearest advocacy statement that reliability headroom should receive compensation. But Niskanen frames this as a deficiency in current market structures (a problem to fix), not as an endorsement of leaving capacity uncompensated.

The dominant critique throughout the advocate literature is economic underutilization — capacity unused when there is a positive price differential — not low average physical utilization. The 2026 LBNL paper by Safavi et al. quantifies the inefficiency: “26% of all net flow on the interfaces we study between 2014 and 2023 displaces relatively cheaper within-region generation with more expensive generation and… most interfaces are regularly underutilized, even when the energy being exchanged is economical.” The Brattle/Willkie Farr report estimates “$50–60 million annually in additional value for every gigawatt of intertie capacity beyond what bilateral trades are currently able to capture.” This frame strongly favors some mechanism for the reliability vintage to flow opportunistically when prices justify it.

The advocate community’s posture toward the federal anchor program will therefore be conditional. The program’s reliability vintage architecture is roughly the kind of infrastructure they advocate for (interregional capacity sized for resource adequacy benefits during stress events), but the program’s design choice to wall the reliability vintage off from commercial dispatch markets directly contradicts their preferred mechanism (compensation through ELCC accreditation plus market-eligible dispatch). Whether the advocate community supports or opposes the program depends substantially on how the proposal is framed and how the reliability vintage’s market posture is described.

The Pacific Intertie analogy is rhetorically two-edged. RMI invokes the Pacific Intertie as a success story for interregional transmission — President Kennedy’s 1961 directive leading to a vital link between California and the Pacific Northwest. The Northwest Power and Conservation Council notes that “most of the power transmitted on the Intertie is surplus to regional needs, but some firm power also is transmitted.” But E3 and NARUC critique that same partly-non-firm structure as Pacific Intertie–era underutilization: “Recent historical data for the operation of existing interregional transmission lines indicate that interregional interties are often underutilized even when flows are most valuable.” The contemporary push for Western EIM, intertie optimization, and SEEM is largely framed as correcting the Pacific Intertie–era inefficiency. The federal anchor program needs to anticipate which framing advocates adopt and be prepared for the opposing rhetorical move.

DOE’s Transmission Facilitation Program is rhetorical infrastructure that doesn’t support the federal anchor program’s reliability vintage design. DOE’s TFP capacity-contract awards (SWIP-N, Aroostook, Cimarron Link, Southline, Southern Spirit) are designed as temporary commercial gap-fillers that DOE intends to resell, not as standing federal reliability reserves. Rob Gramlich praised this design feature in POWER Magazine: “that sequence — allowing for the DOE’s capacity reservation before transmission construction, and then allowing the line’s subscribers to pay back the government — is a notable step forward.” A federal anchor proposal contemplating permanent uncommercialized federal reliability vintage is a structural departure from how the existing TFP works, and advocates have not built rhetorical scaffolding for it.

No major advocate currently endorses deliberately uncommercialized HVDC reliability headroom. A targeted review of Princeton ZERO Lab (Jenkins/Ricks), MIT CEEPR, LBNL EMP, and the broader advocate literature found no paper that models deliberate reservation of HVDC capacity outside wholesale markets as an optimal design choice. There is a genuine literature gap. The federal anchor proposal is operating in a conceptual space the advocacy community has not yet built rhetorical infrastructure around — both an opportunity (to define the framing) and a vulnerability (proponents must do the conceptual work that doesn’t exist elsewhere).

Commercialization-as-Wedge: The Sophisticated Critique

The reliability vintage’s existence creates a structural vulnerability that opponents could exploit through a sophisticated critique distinct from the broad commercialization preference. The argument’s surface form sounds reasonable: 300-500 MW per line of physical capacity sitting unused most of the time invites the suggestion that it should be commercialized through an energy imbalance market — SEEM expansion to include TVA, or bilateral EIM-style arrangements between TVA and PJM/Duke/MISO South across the new physical interconnections. The “wasted infrastructure” framing is politically resonant in cost-conscious environments where any unused asset looks like ratepayer money sitting idle.

But this is a Trojan horse for market integration. The mechanism doesn’t stop at one-time monetization of unused capacity. EIM participation creates regular operational data flows, real-time price signals influencing dispatch, and ongoing operational integration. Over time the boundary between TVA and the destination RTOs becomes more permeable even if TVA’s formal legal independence remains intact. Once TVA is running real-time settlement against PJM or MISO price signals, it’s effectively participating in those markets without formal membership, and formal RTO membership argued as a natural extension of integration that’s already happening becomes considerably easier to make.

The historical analog is BPA’s ongoing pressure to integrate into CAISO’s Western EIM. BPA has held the line on full integration but only through strong institutional commitment, not through any structural feature that prevents pressure from accumulating. The federal anchor program is potentially more exposed than TVA’s existing seams because it creates new physical interconnections where commercialization arguments can be made fresh, without the historical baggage of TVA’s longstanding non-RTO structure. The reliability vintage’s specific framing as “extra capacity beyond contracted commercial use” practically begs the question of why it isn’t commercialized.

Who would make this argument? Several constituencies have alignment with the commercialization framing. The interregional transmission advocate community (described above) prefers commercialized capacity on substantive grounds — they’re not pursuing a market integration agenda per se, but their preferred mechanisms (intertie optimization, ELCC accreditation, EIM participation) all converge on commercial dispatch. RTO administrators and certain FERC constituencies push for expanded market integration as a matter of broader policy. Cost-conscious appropriators look for ways to reduce federal commitment and would find commercialization attractive if it appears to reduce ratepayer or taxpayer burden. IOUs that lose generation business to TVA-acquired federal anchor plants could find commercialization useful as a way to make the program’s structure more vulnerable to future market-integration-driven dilution.

The structural protections built into the program design (statutory reservation of reliability vintage for emergency exchange use only, Federal Anchor Authority dispatch control, TVA dispatch over home-jurisdiction segments, explicit prohibition on participation in commercial dispatch markets) are necessary but not sufficient on their own. Political pressure for commercialization will accumulate over time as the new physical interconnections become routine and as cost-conscious appropriations cycles probe for ways to reduce federal commitment.

The proactive framing required is that the reliability vintage is reserved emergency infrastructure paid for under a defined cost recovery architecture, and that commercialization would compromise the structural integrity of TVA’s market independence in ways that aren’t recovered by the modest monetization the commercialization advocates project. The layered cost recovery architecture is itself the strongest defense because it forecloses the “wasted infrastructure” framing: if hyperscalers are explicitly paying for the reliability vintage as infrastructure they benefit from, ratepayers’ share is bounded, and federal appropriations cover the tail share, the “capacity sitting idle” rhetoric collapses. The capacity isn’t wasted — it’s reserved infrastructure that every party with stake understands and is paying for as such.

Building the Counter-Coalition

The opposition is substantial but not unanimous, and several political forces align favorably with the proposal. Public power trade associations (American Public Power Association, National Rural Electric Cooperative Association) will support the program as a meaningful expansion of public power’s role in federal energy policy. Hyperscaler companies will support the program because it solves their clean firm capacity problem at predictable cost — though they will negotiate on specific terms, particularly the Reserve Fund contribution magnitude and the construction-period advance contributions. The nuclear manufacturing supply chain (Westinghouse and its many subcontractors) will support the program as a major source of new AP1000 orders. National security and AI competitiveness advocates will support the program as a strategic infrastructure response to PRC competition. Climate and decarbonization advocates will support the program for its emissions reduction potential.

Specific states benefit asymmetrically. Tennessee, Alabama, Mississippi, Virginia, North Carolina, and Georgia are direct beneficiaries through the TVA-led Phase 1 pilots. New York and Nebraska benefit through Phase 2 pilots. States with significant nuclear manufacturing supply chains (Pennsylvania, Ohio, South Carolina) benefit through jobs and economic activity. The combined geographic distribution of program benefits should provide a credible base of Congressional support.

The interregional transmission advocate community is a more uncertain coalition member. The advocate community supports the program’s underlying purpose (new interregional capacity addressing resource adequacy needs) but has substantive disagreement with the proposal’s design choice to wall the reliability vintage off from commercial dispatch markets. Whether the advocate community supports or opposes the program depends substantially on how proponents engage with them during the legislative process and how the reliability vintage’s market posture is framed.

The opposition’s most effective angle of attack is the “fairness” argument: the program creates competitive asymmetries between public and private utilities and between IOUs in different regions. The strongest counter is the substantive case that the public-preference principle has worked in U.S. federal power policy for nearly a century, has demonstrated long-term durability, and addresses real market failures that purely private mechanisms have not solved. The opposition’s strongest substantive angle is the “moral hazard” argument: federal absorption of construction risk reduces the incentive for cost discipline. Version 2.0’s POU first-loss absorption layer (100-120% of acquisition price) substantially addresses this concern, as does the hyperscaler Reserve Fund’s first-tier risk absorption above POU absorption.

Neither counter is dispositive, and the political fight will be real. But the substantive case is defensible on its merits, the coalition of supporters is meaningful in scale, and the structural alternatives are each substantially worse than the program this proposal outlines.

Political Risk Mitigation

The opposition forces identified above are real, but each can be partially addressed through specific structural concessions that preserve the proposal’s core architecture while reducing the political cost of opposition. The trade-offs are explicit: every concession that softens opposition complicates the proposal’s structure or dilutes the public-preference principle to some degree. The right mitigation package is one that buys enough political acceptance to enable enactment without unwinding the structural features that make the proposal work.

Mitigating Adjacent IOU Opposition: Minority Ownership Slices

The most significant single mitigation — and the one most likely to convert Phase 1 IOU partners from conditional opponents to active supporters — is to permit those IOUs to take minority ownership slices in the plants alongside TVA as the majority acquirer. This co-ownership variant adds some structural complexity to the federal-balance-sheet construction model, but it can be incorporated as a defined option rather than as the architectural default.

Under this mitigation, the federal government builds the plant on the federal balance sheet as in the base proposal. At commercial operation, the plant is transferred jointly to TVA and one or more IOU minority co-owners at the agreed acquisition price, with TVA holding 60-70% of the plant and IOU partner(s) holding 30-40% collectively. TVA remains the operator and the lead acquirer; the IOU’s minority position is purely economic, providing rate-base ownership in generation without operational responsibility. The IOU’s share of the acquisition price is financed through normal IOU capital structure with state-regulated rate base recovery; TVA’s share is financed through its expanded debt authority.

This gives Southern Company, Duke, and Dominion something substantial: a rate-base position in the new federal nuclear fleet. Vogtle 3 & 4 cost Southern Company roughly 2.5x the original estimate, but Southern still earned regulated return on its share throughout the project’s life. A 30% co-ownership position in a federally-built AP1000 at the original-estimate acquisition price gives Southern an equivalent rate-base position at substantially lower risk and cost than Vogtle, because the IOU’s exposure is bounded by the layered cost recovery architecture. Dominion gets a meaningful nuclear rate-base position without having to navigate Virginia regulatory approval for in-state construction. Duke gets a defined entry into nuclear ownership without bearing first-of-a-kind execution risk.

The complications are real. First, the federal-balance-sheet construction model and the layered cost recovery architecture are structurally simpler with a single POU acquirer. Multiple acquirers require defining how POU first-loss absorption is allocated, how the Reserve Fund disburses to multiple parties, and how state PUC prudency reviews handle a federally-set acquisition price for IOU co-owners. Second, the public-preference principle is partially diluted: IOU rate-base position in generation means some share of operating economics flows to IOU shareholders rather than to public-utility ratepayers. Third, the legislative drafting is more complex because the program must specify how mixed-acquirer projects are handled differently from single-acquirer projects.

The case for accepting these complications is the political acceptance they buy. The minority co-ownership option moves the affected IOUs from being conditional opponents to being aligned supporters, which is a major political shift even if it dilutes the structural purity of the proposal.

Mitigating Non-Adjacent IOU Opposition: Geographic Program Expansion

The non-adjacent IOU opposition is best addressed by Phase 2 geographic expansion. NYPA in New York, NPPD/OPPD/LES in Nebraska, LADWP in California, SRP in Arizona, CPS Energy in Texas, JEA in Florida — each Phase 2 pilot creates IOU participation opportunities in additional regions, reducing the share of IOUs that get nothing from the program. The geographic expansion is parallel to Phase 1, not sequential, so non-adjacent IOU opposition can be mitigated during the initial enactment phase rather than deferred to a later expansion.

Complementary mitigations include tiered IOU eligibility for transmission rate-base position even where the IOU isn’t a destination for federal anchor program deliveries (allowing IOUs to invest in interregional transmission infrastructure with federal credit enhancement, while construction-cost-overrun absorption for plants remains public-utility-only). This expands the IOU constituency that benefits from the program without diluting the cost-overrun absorption layer that’s the core of the public-preference principle.

Constellation and IPP Opposition: No Concessions, Sequenced Engagement

Version 2.0 adopts a different posture toward IPP opposition than Version 1.0 contemplated. The IPP opposition has more credibility on technical grounds than the EEI opposition, but acceding to IPP demands for federal cost-overrun absorption or Reserve Fund eligibility would fundamentally compromise the public-preference principle the program is built on. The decision is that the program’s concession bandwidth is better spent on adjacent IOU minority co-ownership (where the structural integrity can be partially preserved) than on IPP accommodations (where the structural integrity unravels).

The IPP opposition is therefore managed through three mechanisms rather than through structural concessions. First, sequenced engagement: the IPP opposition is most effective if mobilized early. The program’s authorizing legislation can be structured to provide for future Phase 3 review of IPP-related issues, providing IPPs with a defined channel for future engagement without committing to specific accommodations during initial enactment. Second, operating partnership opportunities: federal anchor program plants acquired by TVA could potentially be operated under contract by IPP operators (Constellation has the deepest commercial nuclear operations expertise), with operational decisions remaining subject to public-utility approval, plant ownership remaining with the public utility, and federal cost-overrun absorption flowing only to the public-utility acquirer. Third, supply chain participation: IPPs that participate in the federal anchor program’s nuclear supply chain (construction contracting, fuel cycle services, specialized maintenance) maintain meaningful commercial relationships with the program even without being acquirers.

The honest framing is that the IPP opposition will be substantial and the program will have to absorb it rather than mitigate it through structural accommodation. The public-preference principle requires that direct federal supports flow only to public utilities; any expansion of those supports to IPPs unravels the principle. The trade-off is real political cost during enactment in exchange for structural integrity of the program.

Mitigating State PUC Opposition: State-Level Engagement and Optional Participation

The state PUC opposition is partially addressed by making IOU participation in the program optional at the state level. The Federal Anchor Generation Authority does not compel any IOU to participate. A state PUC that opposes IOU participation can simply not approve the IOU’s request to participate, and the program continues without that IOU. The political effect is that state PUC opposition shifts from being a federal-level lobbying force to being a state-level decision point where each state independently chooses whether to participate.

A complementary mitigation is targeted state-level engagement during the federal legislative process. State PUCs with concerns about the program can be engaged through formal consultation requirements, providing them with structured opportunities to shape program terms in ways that reduce their concerns. NARUC (the National Association of Regulatory Utility Commissioners) can be engaged as a federal-level interlocutor for state PUC concerns.

The package-deal framing developed in the main proposal substantially addresses the substantive grounds on which state PUCs would approve destination IOU participation, particularly for the reliability vintage rate-base absorption layer. Virginia’s VSCC presents the strongest substantive case given Virginia-specific structural pressures (VCEA compliance, PJM capacity volatility, Northern Virginia data center concentration). The other Phase 1 destination states present calibrated versions of similar logic.

Mitigating Interregional Transmission Advocate Opposition

The interregional transmission advocate community presents a different kind of opposition than the IOU/IPP/PUC opposition. The advocates support the program’s underlying purpose (new interregional capacity) but disagree with the proposal’s design choice on reliability vintage commercialization. Mitigation here is partly substantive (engaging with their concerns on the merits) and partly rhetorical (framing the program in ways that fit their existing analytical infrastructure).

The substantive mitigation is to frame the reliability vintage as ELCC-accredited capacity to the extent the program can do so consistent with the design choice to wall it off from commercial dispatch markets. The Brooks/Silverstein/Gramlich June 2025 Grid Strategies report’s analytical framework is compatible with ELCC accreditation that doesn’t require continuous commercial energy flows — accreditation reflecting the reliability vintage’s resource adequacy contribution during stress events. PJM and MISO would need to develop accreditation mechanisms that don’t currently exist (PJM today “attributes very little resource adequacy value to existing interregional transmission and zero value to new interregional transmission”), which is a substantial regulatory ask, but it positions the program within the analytical framework the advocate community has developed rather than outside it.

The rhetorical mitigation is to drop “dark headroom” or “uncommercialized reserve” framing and adopt language the advocate community uses: “ELCC-accredited reliability vintage,” “bidirectional resilience capacity,” “interregional resource adequacy infrastructure.” The conceptual content is the same; the framing makes the proposal legible to Grid Strategies, ACEG, ESIG, Brattle, and Niskanen.

The structural mitigation is to engage the advocate community early for collaborative work on what one of the Stage 4 recommendations in the underlying analysis calls “a white paper on reliability-vintage compensation models.” No such paper currently exists, and the federal anchor proposal could fund or co-author one — filling a literature gap that the advocate community has not yet addressed and giving the advocate community structural ownership of the analytical infrastructure for the federal anchor program’s reliability vintage. The natural co-authors are Pfeifenberger (Brattle), Brooks/Silverstein/Gramlich (Grid Strategies/ACEG), Allen/Reed (Niskanen), and Lasher (ESIG transmission resilience task force).

These mitigations don’t fully eliminate the tension between the program’s design and the advocate community’s preferences, but they substantially reduce the opposition intensity and position the proposal as a structural variant of the advocate community’s general direction rather than as a structural alternative to it.

Mitigating Commercialization-as-Wedge: Statutory Protections Plus Sustained Advocacy

The commercialization-as-wedge critique requires a different mitigation than the broader advocate community engagement because it operates as a sophisticated structural attack rather than as a substantive disagreement.

The principal structural mitigation is statutory protection of the reliability vintage from commercialization. Federal authorizing legislation should explicitly reserve the reliability vintage capacity for emergency exchange use only, with prohibition on participation in commercial dispatch markets (RTOs, EIMs including SEEM expansion, bilateral commercial arrangements). The Federal Anchor Authority dispatch control over cross-jurisdictional reliability vintage segments and TVA dispatch control over home-jurisdiction segments should both be specified statutorily. This makes commercialization require new federal legislation, not just administrative reinterpretation by future program leadership.

The supplementary mitigation is the layered cost recovery architecture itself. The “wasted infrastructure” framing depends on characterizing the reliability vintage as ratepayer money sitting idle; if hyperscalers are explicitly paying for it as reliability infrastructure they benefit from, ratepayers’ share is bounded, and federal appropriations cover the tail share, the framing collapses. The architecture has to be communicated proactively in the program’s written record and in proponent advocacy from the beginning, because reactive responses to commercialization arguments tend to be defensive and unconvincing.

The honest acknowledgment is that even with these mitigations, political pressure for commercialization will accumulate over time. Sustaining the program’s structural integrity will require ongoing political commitment from public power constituencies and from federal program leadership across multiple administrations. This is the same dynamic BPA has navigated for decades with the Pacific Intertie and Western EIM pressure, with mixed but mostly successful results. The federal anchor program should explicitly contemplate that the commercialization pressure will be a permanent feature of the political landscape rather than an opposition to be defeated once and for all.

The Mitigation Package

A practical implementation of these mitigations produces the following package:

The IOU minority co-ownership option is offered as a structural feature of the program from initial enactment, with the structural protections preserving the public-preference principle in modified form. This is the largest single mitigation and the one most likely to convert Phase 1 IOU partners from opponents to supporters.

The Phase 2 geographic expansion to NYPA, Nebraska, LADWP, and others is pursued in parallel with Phase 1, reducing the non-adjacent IOU exclusion. The tiered IOU eligibility for transmission rate-base position (without cost-overrun absorption) is included from initial enactment to expand the IOU constituency without diluting the cost-overrun absorption layer.

IPP opposition is managed without structural concessions through operating partnership opportunities, supply chain participation, and sequenced engagement for future Phase 3 review of IPP-related issues. The decision to spend concession bandwidth on IOU minority co-ownership rather than on IPP accommodation reflects the structural reality that IPP accommodations unravel the public-preference principle in ways that IOU minority co-ownership does not.

State PUC engagement is structured through formal consultation requirements, NARUC interlocution, and optional participation at the state level. The package-deal framing in the main proposal provides the substantive grounds for state PUC approval of destination IOU participation.

The interregional transmission advocate community is engaged through substantive (ELCC accreditation pursued), rhetorical (advocate community language adopted), and structural (collaborative white paper) mechanisms. The objective is to position the program within the advocate community’s analytical infrastructure rather than outside it.

The commercialization-as-wedge critique is managed through statutory protections, the layered cost recovery architecture as proactive framing, and sustained political commitment across administrations.

The cumulative effect of this mitigation package is a program that is structurally more complex than the base proposal but politically more viable. The public-preference principle is preserved in its substantive core while accommodating IOU and PUC participation in roles that align their interests with the program’s success. The political fight will still be real, but the coalition of supporters is larger and the opposition is narrower than the base proposal would produce.

The honest assessment is that some version of this mitigation package is likely necessary for enactment. The base proposal — purely public-utility eligibility with no accommodation — is structurally cleaner but politically harder. The mitigation package described here represents a reasonable compromise between structural integrity and political achievability, with particular attention to the commercialization tension that Version 2.0 newly addresses.
