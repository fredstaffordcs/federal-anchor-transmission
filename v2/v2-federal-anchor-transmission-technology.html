Federal Anchor Transmission: Technology, Ownership, and Regulatory Architecture

Companion document to “Federal Anchor Transmission: A Proposal for Interregional Buildout Through Federally-Built Clean Firm Generation.”

Fred Stafford · fred@publicpowerreview.org Version 2.0 · May 20, 2026

This companion document develops the transmission technology, segment ownership, and FERC regulatory architecture that accompany the main proposal. The main document establishes the federal-balance-sheet construction model for clean firm generation and the segmented-ownership framework for anchor transmission, drawing on the Pacific Intertie precedent. This document specifies what those design principles imply for the specific Phase 1 pilot lines: which transmission technology to use, which entity should own which segment, who should have operational dispatch authority, and what FERC and federal legislative architecture is required to make each pilot workable. The recommendations developed here are intended to inform the program’s authorizing legislation and the associated tariff filings before affected RTOs and FERC.

Executive Summary

  • Three of four Phase 1 pilots should be ±525 kV HVDC; only the shortest, fully intra-SERC line (Bellefonte → Atlanta, ~250 mi, both non-RTO) should be 500 kV AC, and even that line’s endpoints should be designed to accommodate future back-to-back DC retrofit.

  • The technology choice is structural, not just engineering. HVDC’s controlled point-to-point flow eliminates loop-flow entanglement with neighboring RTO market mechanics, preserving TVA’s cost-of-service dispatch autonomy across the multi-decade operating life of assets that may outlast multiple regulatory regimes.

  • Each anchor line uses a segmented ownership architecture with three potential owners: the anchor public power operator (TVA, NYPA, NPPD, etc.) owns the segment within its home jurisdiction; the federal anchor authority owns cross-jurisdictional segments and the receiving converter station; the destination IOU owns the downstream AC leg into its data center load.

  • Federal ownership of cross-jurisdictional segments avoids the legal lift of expanding each public power operator’s territorial authority through state legislatures. The federal anchor authority becomes a reusable build-phase service institution — siting, permitting, eminent domain, construction-phase financing — that scales across the entire public power acquirer base.

  • Operational dispatch is delegated to the anchor public power operator under a long-term operating agreement, separating economic ownership from operational control. TVA dispatches the entire HVDC link as an integrated asset even though the federal authority owns the cross-jurisdictional portion.

  • The receiving converter station uses federal majority ownership with IOU minority economic stake (approximately 35 percent). The IOU’s stake is purely economic — a rate-base position with associated regulated return, but no operational control rights. This addresses the coalition equity problem for Dominion (whose downstream AC segment is much shorter than Southern’s or Duke’s in the AC pilot) without surrendering structural autonomy at the dispatch control point.

  • The IOU-versus-POU asymmetry in converter station ownership is a feature of the public-preference principle, not a defect. A hypothetical Phase 2 pilot with a public power destination utility could appropriately own and dispatch its receiving converter station outright, as LADWP does on the Pacific Intertie; IOU destinations cannot, because their institutional logic isn’t aligned with the program’s structural goals in the same way.

  • The architecture preserves the Pacific Intertie’s central principle — cost allocation settled by ownership, with no federal-level allocation methodology required — but is structurally more complex than the Intertie due to weakened federal siting authority, contested state-level utility regulation, and IOU rather than POU destinations.

  • FERC regulatory lift scales with each pilot’s complexity. The intra-SERC AC pilot (Bellefonte) requires only standard wholesale rate filings and bilateral transmission service. The intra-SERC HVDC pilot (Hartsville) adds federal anchor transmission tariff treatment and operational delegation approval. The cross-RTO HVDC pilots (Clinch River into PJM, Yellow Creek into MISO South) additionally require RTO tariff revisions, federal eminent domain authority, and preemption of destination-state CPCN authority.

  • The legislative package required is substantial but internally coherent. Core elements include statutory creation of the Federal Anchor Generation and Transmission Authority, a renewed Section 216 backstop siting authority with federal eminent domain, FERC tariff authority for federal anchor transmission as a defined category, NERC coordination provisions, and a narrow TVA Act fence carve-out for wholesale sales of federally-anchored generation.

  • Some version of this architecture is likely necessary for the anchor program to deliver substantial new interregional clean firm capacity while preserving the structural autonomy of public power across multi-decade asset lives. Simpler architectures (pure AC, simple Pacific Intertie ownership, full delegation to destination IOUs) would be easier to enact but would surrender the structural protections that make the program meaningful for public power’s long-term institutional position.

The Transmission Technology Choice

For Eastern Interconnection bulk transmission at the distances and capacities relevant to the anchor program, the practical technology choices are 500 kV alternating current (TVA’s existing backbone standard, and the dominant standard across SERC), 765 kV alternating current (AEP’s signature standard, more efficient at longer distances and higher transferred capacity), or high-voltage direct current at ±500 to ±525 kV using the modern Voltage Source Converter standard (the technology used by Champlain Hudson Power Express, SOO Green, and Dominion’s proposed Heritage-Mosby line). Each technology has well-understood physical, operational, and economic properties; the choice among them depends on distance, right-of-way constraints, the technology of the receiving utility’s existing grid, and a set of structural considerations that are easier to overlook but matter more than the engineering analysis alone suggests.

The basic engineering economics favor AC for shorter lines and HVDC for longer lines, with a break-even distance that depends on whether the line is overhead or underground. For overhead lines, the typical break-even is in the range of 400 to 500 miles — HVDC’s lower per-mile losses and narrower right-of-way begin to compensate for the substantial capital cost of the two converter stations required at the line’s endpoints (each converter station pair for a 2,500 MW class line runs approximately $300 to $500 million). For underground lines, the break-even is far shorter, perhaps 30 to 50 miles, because underground AC at high voltage is extraordinarily expensive (the cables must manage substantial reactive power and require active compensation), while underground HVDC is mature, cost-competitive, and routinely deployed.

The operational properties of the two technologies differ in ways that affect more than just engineering cost. AC power flow follows impedance rather than contract paths; a contracted transaction between two points on an AC grid distributes physically across all parallel paths, with the flow on any specific path determined by the grid’s impedance characteristics rather than by the commercial agreement. This is the well-known phenomenon of loop flow, and it has both operational and commercial consequences. Operationally, loop flow can cause unscheduled flows on third-party utilities’ lines, which must be settled retroactively and can disrupt other utilities’ dispatch and reliability planning. Commercially, the unscheduled flows create accounting complications and sometimes financial disputes between utilities that aren’t direct parties to the underlying transaction.

HVDC sidesteps this entirely. A controlled HVDC link acts as a precisely-dispatchable generator at one end and a precisely-dispatchable load at the other. Flow on the link follows the dispatch command, not impedance. There is no loop flow, no unscheduled-flow settlement requirement, and the commercial accounting is clean — DC effectively creates a contractual point-to-point interface between two AC systems, even when those systems are synchronously connected through the broader Eastern Interconnection. This property is why several of the most prominent recent HVDC projects in the United States — SOO Green between MISO and PJM, Grain Belt Express across SPP and MISO and PJM, Champlain Hudson Power Express between Quebec and NYISO — are HVDC even where the systems they connect are synchronously compatible. The HVDC choice in those cases is about flow control across market and operational boundaries, not about asynchronous physics.

For a public power program, the flow-control property of HVDC has a structural significance beyond its engineering convenience. When TVA wholesales power to a neighboring utility on AC, TVA’s dispatch decisions become physically entangled with the dispatch decisions of every utility in the surrounding region whose lines carry parallel flow. The settlement, operational coordination, and reliability planning protocols that accompany these flows create ongoing institutional touch points between TVA and its neighbors, many of which are RTO members operating under FERC-coordinated market structures that TVA has historically remained outside. Each such touch point is a small vector of pressure toward greater institutional integration — toward TVA harmonizing its operational and market structures with the surrounding RTOs, eventually joining one of them, and surrendering the cost-of-service-based dispatch autonomy that has defined TVA’s institutional identity since 1933. HVDC, by eliminating the operational entanglement at its source, preserves the institutional autonomy that AC architecture quietly erodes.

This is the central insight that distinguishes a public-power framing of the transmission technology question from a pure-engineering framing. The engineering question asks which technology minimizes capital and operating cost over the asset’s life. The public-power question asks which technology best preserves the institutional autonomy of the public power operator across the operating life of an asset that may outlast multiple regulatory regimes. The answers usually overlap — both framings favor HVDC at long distances and AC at short distances — but at the margin, the public-power framing biases toward HVDC more aggressively than the engineering framing does, and it weights structural considerations that don’t appear on engineering cost spreadsheets.

Line-by-Line Analysis

The proposal’s Phase 1 pilots comprise three TVA-anchored lines plus the Phase 1+ Yellow Creek expansion, each with distinct characteristics that drive the technology choice. The analysis below works through each in turn.

Bellefonte to Metro Atlanta, Approximately 250 Miles

The Bellefonte to Atlanta line is comfortably 500 kV AC territory on engineering grounds alone. TVA already operates a 500 kV switchyard at Bellefonte, dating from the originally planned twin-PWR construction in the 1970s, and Southern Company operates 500 kV across north Georgia. The synchronous AC tie is straightforward, and a single circuit can carry 2,000 to 3,000 MW of firm capacity, which is well-matched to the anticipated offtake. The line traverses populated areas of north Alabama and north Georgia where right-of-way acquisition has historical precedent and reasonably well-understood costs.

Southern Company is currently non-RTO, with its own balancing authority and reliability coordinator. There is no market-seam crossing under current conditions, and the cross-utility operational coordination protocols between TVA and Southern are well-established. The flow-control case for HVDC is therefore weaker for this line than for any of the others. On engineering grounds, AC is the right choice, with a single-circuit 500 kV AC line carrying 1,000 to 1,500 MW of TVA-Southern wholesale capacity, structured as a synchronous interconnection.

The public-power structural argument is not absent, however. Southern Company’s non-RTO status is not guaranteed to persist over the asset’s 50+ year operating life. FERC has periodically pushed for greater RTO membership, and various policy and market developments could change Southern’s institutional posture in ways that aren’t predictable from current conditions. If Southern joins PJM in 2035 or 2045, the Bellefonte-Atlanta AC line becomes a vector for TVA’s dispatch becoming entangled with PJM market mechanics, exactly the situation HVDC would prevent. The structural insurance argument for HVDC on this line is therefore real, even if the immediate engineering case favors AC.

The recommended approach is to build the line as 500 kV AC on engineering and capital cost grounds, but to design the Bellefonte and Southern receiving substations to accommodate a back-to-back DC tie installation in the future if Southern’s institutional status changes in ways that warrant it. This is a modest design accommodation — primarily a question of substation site planning and electrical compatibility — that preserves the option to add structural autonomy protection later without committing to the HVDC capital cost now. The cost premium for design-stage accommodation is far smaller than the cost premium for a retrofitted DC tie, and the option value is meaningful.

Hartsville to Charlotte and Research Triangle, Approximately 350 Miles

The Hartsville to Charlotte line is in the ambiguous zone where the engineering economics of AC and HVDC are roughly comparable, and the structural considerations push the decision. At 350 miles overhead, 500 kV AC works but is approaching the distance where 765 kV AC becomes meaningfully more efficient in losses and capacity, and where ±525 kV HVDC becomes competitive on lifecycle cost despite the converter station capital premium. Duke Energy operates 500 kV across its Carolinas footprint but has no 765 kV infrastructure, so a 765 kV corridor would require step-down at the Duke boundary, which substantially complicates the design and largely negates the 765 kV efficiency advantage.

Duke is currently non-RTO. The structural-insurance argument for HVDC applies with the same logic as for the Bellefonte line — Duke’s non-RTO status is not guaranteed to persist, and HVDC architecture preserves TVA’s dispatch autonomy regardless of how Duke’s institutional posture evolves. The argument is stronger here than for the Bellefonte line because the distance is greater (the AC alternative is less attractive on its own terms) and because Duke’s institutional trajectory is harder to predict — Duke has periodically signaled openness to RTO membership in ways Southern has not.

The flow-control argument for HVDC also applies more strongly at 350 miles than at 250 miles. Loop flow effects from a 1,500 MW AC injection at 350 miles distribute across more of the Eastern Interconnection than the same injection at 250 miles, creating more operational coordination requirements with intervening utilities. The bilateral settlement complications grow with distance, and HVDC’s clean point-to-point architecture becomes more valuable.

The recommended approach is ±525 kV HVDC for the Hartsville to Charlotte line, with the receiving converter station located at a Duke 500 kV substation reasonably close to the Charlotte-RTP load center. The capital cost premium over AC is real but manageable in the context of the overall pilot economics, and the combined engineering, flow-control, and structural-autonomy arguments together support the HVDC choice even though the engineering case alone would be marginal.

Clinch River to Northern Virginia, Approximately 450 Miles

The Clinch River to Northern Virginia line is the clearest HVDC case in the pilot portfolio. At 450 miles overhead, the engineering economics favor HVDC on lifecycle cost grounds alone — lower line losses, narrower right-of-way (a single approximately 150-foot HVDC corridor versus the approximately 200-foot corridor required for double-circuit 500 kV AC), and higher firm transfer capacity per circuit. The receiving terminus in Northern Virginia is the largest data center market in the world, with right-of-way acquisition in Loudoun and Prince William counties contested and expensive, which further favors HVDC’s narrower corridor.

The line crosses the TVA-PJM market seam at the Tennessee-Virginia border. Dominion Energy is a full PJM member, with its dispatch coordinated through PJM’s locational marginal pricing market structure. An AC interconnection at this seam would create substantial loop flow effects across PJM and would entangle TVA’s dispatch with PJM market mechanics through every cross-RC settlement cycle. This is the clearest case in the pilot portfolio where structural autonomy is at stake.

The recommended approach is ±525 kV HVDC for the full route, with converter stations at Clinch River and at a Dominion 500 kV substation reasonably close to the Northern Virginia load center (likely in the existing Dominion bulk transmission ring around Loudoun County). The HVDC link operates as a single controllable interface from TVA generation to the doorstep of the load, with TVA dispatching the link as part of its integrated system rather than as a PJM-coordinated asset. Dominion handles the final short AC leg from the receiving converter station to the data center cluster using its existing 500 kV network, which is well-developed in the Northern Virginia area.

The HVDC choice on this line is not a marginal decision; it is structurally essential to the program’s autonomy architecture. The Clinch River pilot establishes the precedent that federal anchor generation can serve PJM-area load through non-RTO public power operation without TVA being absorbed into PJM market structures. That precedent matters for the broader program because it determines whether other public power acquirers (NYPA, NPPD, LADWP, others) can use similar architectures to serve RTO-area loads while preserving their own institutional autonomy. AC architecture on the Clinch River line would foreclose this option at the most consequential pilot.

Yellow Creek to Central Mississippi, Approximately 300 Miles

The Yellow Creek to Central Mississippi line is the Phase 1+ expansion candidate, serving the growing data center cluster in Entergy Mississippi’s central-Mississippi service area, where Meta’s Forest, MS data center and Amazon’s Madison County investment have established a substantial concentrated load. At 300 miles, the line is at the lower end of the distance range where HVDC becomes attractive on engineering grounds, with the AC alternative still competitive on capital cost.

The decisive consideration is that the line crosses the TVA-MISO South market seam. Entergy Mississippi is a MISO South member, and MISO operates an LMP-based market that is structurally similar to PJM’s. An AC interconnection at this seam would create loop flow effects and operational entanglement with MISO market mechanics, replicating the structural autonomy concerns of the Clinch River line in the MISO context. The structural autonomy argument for HVDC applies to this line for the same reasons it applies to the Clinch River line, even though the distance is shorter.

The recommended approach is ±525 kV HVDC for the full route, with converter stations at Yellow Creek and at an Entergy Mississippi substation reasonably close to the central-Mississippi load center. The architecture parallels the Clinch River pilot in all relevant respects — federal authority owns the cross-jurisdictional segment, TVA dispatches the link under operational delegation, Entergy handles the AC leg downstream. The Yellow Creek pilot demonstrates the federal anchor program’s portability across multiple cross-RTO seams (PJM via Clinch River, MISO South via Yellow Creek), which is a meaningful proof-of-concept for the broader Phase 2 expansion.

The Pattern Across the Pilots

The four pilots produce a portfolio that is three-quarters HVDC and one-quarter AC, with the AC line (Bellefonte to Atlanta) designed to accommodate future HVDC retrofit if conditions warrant. This is more HVDC-heavy than a pure engineering analysis would produce, reflecting the public-power structural considerations that weight HVDC’s autonomy-preservation properties more heavily than the engineering case alone justifies. The cumulative capital cost premium for the HVDC bias, relative to a pure-engineering portfolio, is meaningful but not large — perhaps 8 to 12 percent of total transmission capital across the four pilots, well within the program’s overall budget envelope.

The fleet standardization benefits of the HVDC-heavy portfolio are substantial. TVA’s operating organization develops HVDC operational competence as a core capability across three of four pilot lines, rather than as a specialized skill for one or two. The federal anchor authority’s institutional capacity concentrates on a single technology platform. The equipment vendor supply chain (Siemens, GE, Hitachi for converter stations, Prysmian and similar for cable) develops sustained relationships with the anchor program rather than serving the pilots as one-off projects. These institutional and supply-chain benefits compound across the pilots in ways that affect both project economics and the program’s long-term operational reliability.

Segment Ownership and Operational Control

The Pacific Intertie precedent that the main proposal draws on settles transmission cost allocation through ownership: each utility owns its segment, recovers capital through its own ratemaking mechanism, and dispatches its segment under its own institutional rules. For the anchor program, this principle holds in modified form. The modifications are necessary because the anchor lines cross jurisdictional boundaries that the Pacific Intertie did not, and because the structural autonomy considerations developed above require a separation between asset ownership and operational control that the Pacific Intertie did not require.

The proposed architecture has three primary segment owners across each anchor line: the anchor public power operator (TVA for the Phase 1 pilots), the federal anchor authority, and the destination investor-owned utility. Each owner holds a defined category of segment based on where that segment sits and what kind of institutional capacity is appropriate for owning it.

Anchor Public Power Operator: Home-Jurisdiction Segment

TVA owns the segment of each anchor line within its statutory service territory, including any converter station at the TVA-side endpoint. For the Bellefonte to Atlanta line, this is the Alabama portion of the AC line plus the Bellefonte sending substation. For the Hartsville, Clinch River, and Yellow Creek pilots, this is the Tennessee portion of the HVDC line plus the sending converter station. TVA finances these segments through its existing or expanded bond authority, rate-bases them through its standard wholesale rate mechanism, and dispatches them as integrated components of its existing transmission system.

This portion of the architecture requires no new federal authority for TVA. TVA’s existing statutory authority to own and operate transmission within its service area applies directly. The TVA Act amendment to the statutory debt cap that the main proposal already contemplates is sufficient to accommodate the additional borrowing needed to finance the home-jurisdiction segments alongside the plant acquisitions and other TVA capital needs.

For NYPA, NPPD, LADWP, and other Phase 2 public power acquirers, the home-jurisdiction segment ownership similarly operates under the entity’s existing state-chartered or municipal authority. Each acquirer owns and operates its home-jurisdiction segment under existing institutional capacity, with no novel statutory authority required for territorial expansion.

Federal Anchor Authority: Cross-Jurisdictional Segment

The federal anchor authority owns the segment of each anchor line that crosses outside the anchor public power operator’s home jurisdiction, plus the receiving converter station (where applicable). For the Bellefonte to Atlanta line, the federal authority’s role is minimal because the line is internal to two contiguous utility territories with a clean border at the Alabama-Georgia state line; the federal authority’s primary contribution is siting and permitting support rather than asset ownership. For the HVDC pilots (Hartsville, Clinch River, Yellow Creek), the federal authority owns the substantial cross-jurisdictional portion of each line, including the full HVDC route from the TVA boundary to the receiving converter station and the converter station itself.

The federal anchor authority’s institutional role is build-phase capability concentrated in a single federal institution that scales across the entire public power acquirer base. The authority handles siting, permitting, federal eminent domain (under the renewed Section 216 authority discussed below), construction-phase financing, project management, and ongoing asset stewardship for the cross-jurisdictional segments it owns. Its institutional knowledge compounds across projects rather than being locked into specific assets, and its multi-program portfolio supports sustained operational capability between individual pilot timelines.

The federal authority is not, however, the operational dispatcher of the assets it owns. Operational dispatch is delegated to the anchor public power operator under a long-term operating agreement (discussed below). The federal authority’s ongoing role with respect to operations is oversight rather than control — ensuring safety, security, NERC reliability compliance, and federal financial accountability for the asset, without directing its day-to-day dispatch.

This institutional model is broadly analogous to how federal land management agencies handle assets under lessee operation: the agency holds title and oversees use, while operations are conducted by partner institutions under defined agreements. The model is structurally cleaner than the alternatives (federal ownership with federal operations, or anchor POU ownership of cross-jurisdictional assets) because it matches institutional capabilities to institutional roles. The federal authority is well-positioned for cross-jurisdictional siting and ownership; the anchor POU is well-positioned for operations. Separating these aligns the structure with the underlying institutional competencies rather than forcing one institution to do both.

Destination Investor-Owned Utility: Downstream AC Segment

The destination IOU (Southern Company for Bellefonte to Atlanta, Duke Energy for Hartsville to Charlotte, Dominion Energy for Clinch River to Northern Virginia, Entergy Mississippi for Yellow Creek to Central Mississippi) owns the AC infrastructure from the receiving converter station (for the HVDC pilots) or the state border substation (for the AC pilot) to the data center load. This includes both the AC delivery line itself and the substantial AC system reinforcement infrastructure needed to integrate the new injection into the IOU’s existing grid — new substations, reconductoring, dynamic VAR compensation, system protection upgrades.

The IOU rate-bases these segments through its state-regulated retail ratemaking process, with cost recovery flowing through the retail PPA written to the hyperscaler offtaker. The IOU operates these segments as part of its normal grid operations, with full dispatch authority over the AC portion of its territory. This is the standard utility transmission ownership model and requires no novel regulatory authority.

The asymmetry across the IOUs created by the technology choice (Southern’s segment is much longer than Dominion’s because the Bellefonte line is AC end-to-end while the Clinch River line is HVDC with a federal middle) is addressed through the receiving converter station arrangement discussed in the next section, which gives Dominion a rate-base position in the converter station to offset its shorter downstream AC segment. The principle is to maintain rough rate-base parity across the Phase 1 IOU partners despite the technological differences across the pilots, so that no single IOU is structurally disadvantaged relative to the others and the coalition holds together politically.

The Receiving Converter Station: Federal Ownership with IOU Minority Stake

The receiving converter station on each HVDC pilot is the structurally novel asset. It is the point where the HVDC link’s controllability gets handed off to the receiving IOU’s AC system, and the entity that owns and dispatches it controls when and how the federally-anchored power enters the destination grid. This is more than just regulated capital; it is the operational control point for the HVDC architecture’s structural autonomy properties.

If the IOU owned and dispatched the receiving converter station outright, the IOU would have de facto operational authority over TVA’s exports, which would undermine the structural autonomy that the HVDC architecture was chosen to deliver. The IOU’s institutional logic — shareholder return, state PUC oversight, alignment with the IOU’s commercial position within its RTO — is not aligned with the federal anchor program’s structural goals in the way the anchor POU’s institutional logic is. Letting the IOU dispatch the converter station would surrender the autonomy benefit at the most critical handoff point.

If the federal anchor authority owned and dispatched the converter station outright, the IOU would have no rate-base position in the asset, which would create a coalition equity problem across the Phase 1 IOU partners. Southern and Duke rate-base their full transmission segments (Southern under the AC pilot, Duke under the HVDC pilot with the converter station included in the rate-base discussion); Dominion would rate-base only the short downstream AC leg, which is a substantially smaller asset than what the other IOUs receive. This asymmetry is large enough to fracture the IOU coalition the program depends on for political support.

The recommended structure addresses both concerns through a separation of economic and operational ownership. The federal anchor authority owns 60-70 percent of the receiving converter station and retains full operational dispatch authority. The destination IOU holds a minority economic stake of 30-40 percent, rate-based through state retail ratemaking, with no operational control rights. Capital recovery flows through two parallel mechanisms: the federal authority recovers its majority share through wholesale transmission charges to the IOU, passed through to the hyperscaler PPA; the IOU recovers its minority share through standard state-regulated retail ratemaking, also passed through to the hyperscaler PPA. The IOU’s stake is purely economic — a regulated capital position with associated return on equity, comparable in economic terms to its rate-base position in other transmission infrastructure, but with no operational role.

The operational dispatch of the converter station is held by the federal anchor authority and delegated to the anchor POU under the long-term operating agreement (discussed below). The combination of federal majority ownership and federal operational authority, with IOU economic participation, preserves the autonomy protection that HVDC architecture was chosen to deliver while addressing the coalition equity concern that pure federal ownership would create.

This structure is not perfectly clean. It introduces complexity that the Pacific Intertie precedent did not have — the Intertie’s ownership pattern is straightforward two-party at each line, with each owner holding both economic and operational control over its segment. The receiving converter station arrangement requires more careful legislative drafting to specify how economic participation and operational control are separated, how state regulatory authority interfaces with federal operational authority, and how disputes are resolved. The complications are real, but they are the cost of accommodating the political reality that the Phase 1 IOU partners need rate-base positions sufficient to keep them in the supporter coalition, in a structural context where the natural Pacific Intertie pattern doesn’t quite fit.

The IOU-versus-POU asymmetry created by this structure is worth naming explicitly. A hypothetical Phase 2 pilot with a public power destination utility — NYPA receiving federally-anchored generation in upstate New York, for example — would not need the same converter station ownership architecture, because NYPA’s institutional logic is aligned with the program’s goals and NYPA could appropriately own and dispatch the receiving converter station outright. The Phase 1 IOU partners are structurally on a different footing than Phase 2 public power partners would be, and that asymmetry is a feature of the public-preference principle, not a defect of the design. The public-preference principle preserves a meaningful institutional distinction between public power and investor-owned utilities; the converter station ownership separation is one expression of that distinction at the transmission level.

Operational Delegation: Anchor POU Dispatches the Federal Segment

The federal anchor authority delegates operational dispatch of its segments to the anchor public power operator under a long-term operating agreement. For each HVDC pilot, this means TVA dispatches the entire HVDC link (from the TVA-side converter station, across the federally-owned cross-jurisdictional portion, to the receiving converter station) as a single integrated asset. There is no operational handoff at the state line or at the segment ownership boundary; TVA controls the dispatch instructions for the full HVDC link, with the federal authority’s role being asset stewardship rather than day-to-day operations.

The operating agreement specifies the terms of this delegation in detail. TVA dispatches the assets subject to defined federal oversight (NERC reliability standards, federal security requirements, federal financial accountability for the asset), with a long initial term (perhaps 40 years) and renewal provisions that protect TVA’s operational reliance on the assets over their full useful life. The agreement includes breach provisions that limit the federal authority’s ability to unilaterally withdraw operational delegation without due process, and dispute resolution mechanisms that prevent operational disagreements from disrupting reliable system operation. The agreement is approved as part of the federal anchor authority’s tariff filing at FERC and is binding on subsequent federal administrations.

This delegation structure achieves the dispatch coherence that the structural autonomy argument requires (TVA controls the dispatch decisions for the entire HVDC link, including the portions inside Dominion’s, Duke’s, or Entergy Mississippi’s territories) without requiring TVA to actually own assets outside its statutory service area. The legal lift of expanding TVA’s territorial authority is avoided; TVA’s institutional reach extends through operations rather than through ownership. From an operational standpoint, the difference is invisible — TVA dispatches the assets either way; from a legal and political standpoint, the difference is meaningful — the federal authority handles the cross-jurisdictional ownership lift that TVA, NYPA, NPPD, and other public power acquirers would each face on their own otherwise.

The same delegation structure applies to Phase 2 pilots with non-TVA anchor POUs. NYPA dispatches the federal segment of an anchor line crossing into New Jersey, Pennsylvania, or Connecticut; NPPD dispatches the federal segment of an anchor line crossing into Iowa, Missouri, or Kansas; LADWP dispatches the federal segment of an anchor line operating in the broader Western Interconnection. Each anchor POU operates the full federally-anchored transmission system as if it were integrated with its own grid, with federal asset ownership as the legal mechanism that makes cross-jurisdictional operations possible without requiring each POU to obtain its own territorial expansion authority.

How This Differs from the Pacific Intertie

The Pacific Intertie precedent that the main proposal cites is genuinely instructive, but the architecture developed in this companion document differs from the Intertie in several important respects that deserve explicit acknowledgment.

The Pacific Intertie has segmented ownership with each utility owning its own segment within its territory — BPA owns the northern portion in BPA-served Oregon, LADWP and its consortium partners own the southern portion in Southern California. Both endpoints of the HVDC line sit within their owner’s territory; there is no third-party owner of a middle segment, and there is no separation of economic and operational ownership at any point on the line. LADWP both owns the Sylmar converter station and dispatches it; BPA both owns the Celilo converter station and dispatches it.

The anchor program architecture differs in two respects. First, the federal anchor authority owns the cross-jurisdictional middle segment of each HVDC pilot, which is a structural feature the Intertie does not have. The Intertie’s southern terminus is in LADWP’s territory, and LADWP is a public power entity with institutional logic aligned with BPA’s at the northern terminus; there is no need for a federal middle because both endpoints sit naturally with public power owners. The anchor program’s HVDC pilots terminate at IOU substations in IOU territories where IOU ownership of the converter station would surrender structural autonomy, and the federal middle exists to handle this case. Second, the receiving converter station ownership is separated from operational control on the anchor program lines — federal majority ownership, IOU minority economic participation, federal operational dispatch authority delegated to the anchor POU. The Intertie has no analog to this separation because both converter stations are owned by public power entities that appropriately hold both economic and operational ownership.

These differences are responses to two structural factors that the Pacific Intertie did not face. The Intertie was built in an era when federal infrastructure ownership was politically uncontroversial and when the institutional landscape was simpler. Federal siting authority was reliably available; states cooperated rather than obstructed; LADWP was both the receiving utility and a willing federal partner with operational sophistication. The Intertie’s structural design could be simple because the political environment was permissive. The anchor program operates in a more constrained environment, with weakened federal siting authority, more contested state-level utility regulation, and destination utilities that are IOUs rather than public power entities. The architectural complications in the anchor program — federal middle segments, separated economic and operational ownership — are responses to that more constrained environment.

The underlying logic of the Pacific Intertie nonetheless holds. Cost allocation is settled by ownership, with each owner recovering capital through its own ratemaking mechanism; no federal-level cost allocation methodology is required, no inter-utility benefit-share dispute occurs. The anchor program preserves this principle through the segmented ownership architecture even as the specific ownership pattern is more complex than the Intertie’s. The implementation is more complicated, but the structural logic is the same: ownership settles cost; segments meet at boundaries; each owner has a clear institutional stake in the asset’s continued operation.

FERC Architecture and Regulatory Requirements

The federal anchor program requires substantial development of the FERC regulatory framework. Each pilot makes different demands on the existing regulatory architecture, with the cross-RTO pilots requiring the most novel arrangements. The analysis below works through what is needed for each pilot, building from the FERC-easiest to the FERC-hardest.

Bellefonte to Atlanta: Standard Wholesale and Bilateral Framework

The Bellefonte to Atlanta line operates within the existing bilateral framework between TVA and Southern Company. Required FERC actions are limited to standard wholesale rate filings under Section 205 of the Federal Power Act for TVA’s wholesale sale of federally-anchored generation to Southern, plus updated transmission service arrangements under TVA’s existing transmission service framework and Southern’s existing Open Access Transmission Tariff. Reliability coordination across the TVA and Southern reliability coordinator functions is handled under existing NERC bilateral protocols.

No RTO membership change is required for either utility. No federal preemption of state CPCN authority is required, on the assumption that Tennessee and Georgia approve their respective segments under existing state processes (both states have meaningful interests in the line being built — Tennessee for the rate-base position and the federal anchor investment, Georgia for the data center load capacity expansion). The federal anchor authority’s role on this line is primarily siting and permitting support during the build phase, with no ongoing asset ownership requiring tariff treatment.

This is the FERC-easiest of the pilots and could proceed through standard regulatory processes with modest tariff updates rather than requiring novel regulatory architecture.

Hartsville to Charlotte: Federal Anchor Transmission Tariff Framework

The Hartsville to Charlotte HVDC line introduces the federally-owned cross-jurisdictional segment and the operational delegation arrangement, which require novel FERC tariff treatment. The federal anchor authority files a tariff under Section 205 establishing the rate treatment of its North Carolina segment plus the receiving converter station, with rates structured either as cost-of-service (covering the federal acquisition price plus operational and stewardship costs) or as negotiated rates under FERC’s merchant transmission framework adapted for federal ownership. The operational delegation arrangement with TVA is incorporated into the federal authority’s tariff and requires FERC approval as a related operational agreement.

TVA’s dispatch of the federally-owned segment requires updated transmission service arrangements that recognize TVA’s expanded operational role beyond its traditional service area. The TVA tariff is updated to incorporate the delegation arrangement; the federal authority’s tariff specifies the same arrangement from the federal asset owner’s perspective. The two tariffs are designed to be consistent, with FERC review ensuring no jurisdictional gaps or conflicts.

NERC functional registration is updated to specify how reliability obligations are allocated across the federal authority, TVA, and Duke Energy. The federal authority is most appropriately registered as a Transmission Owner with TVA registered as the Transmission Operator under the delegation agreement; Duke remains the Transmission Owner and Operator for the AC leg downstream and for its reliability coordination functions. Cross-RC coordination protocols are updated to include the federal authority’s oversight role alongside TVA’s and Duke’s existing operational coordination.

This is FERC-novel architecture but builds on existing precedents (Power Marketing Administration tariff frameworks, NERC functional registration flexibility, FERC approval of operational agreements between different transmission entities). The legislative architecture provides FERC with explicit authority to approve the structures as defined categories, which streamlines the regulatory process and reduces the risk that FERC fits the anchor program into existing tariff frameworks that weren’t designed for it.

Clinch River to Northern Virginia: Cross-RTO Seam Architecture

The Clinch River to Northern Virginia HVDC line is the FERC-hardest of the pilots because it crosses the TVA-PJM seam and the receiving utility is a full PJM member. Required FERC actions extend beyond the Hartsville case to include PJM tariff revisions, cross-RTO operational coordination, and federal preemption of certain state regulatory authority.

PJM’s tariff must be revised to accommodate the federal anchor transmission category. The revisions specify how federal anchor transmission interacts with PJM markets, scheduling, and dispatch. The cleanest framework treats the HVDC link as fixed-schedule firm delivery similar to how Champlain Hudson Power Express will operate under its NYSERDA contract — the link delivers contracted power to defined PJM load (Dominion’s hyperscaler PPAs), with PJM scheduling the AC integration at the receiving converter station but not having dispatch authority over the HVDC link itself. The dispatch instruction comes from TVA under the operational delegation arrangement; PJM accommodates the resulting injection as exogenous to its LMP-based dispatch.

The PJM tariff revision is filed by PJM under Section 205, with FERC review ensuring the revision is non-discriminatory and consistent with the open access requirements of Order 888 and Order 890. PJM may resist the revision because it constrains PJM’s operational authority within its footprint; FERC’s authority under the federal anchor legislation should include explicit direction to PJM to file the revision and ongoing oversight of its implementation.

Federal eminent domain authority is critical for this line. The Virginia State Corporation Commission has substantial influence over Dominion’s regulatory environment, and obtaining VSCC approval for a federally-owned line through Virginia is uncertain. The renewed Section 216 backstop authority must be substantive enough to actually clear hostile state action, with eminent domain authority included and judicial review limited and expedited. This is one of the most contested pieces of the federal legislation; the political fight over the scope of federal preemption will be substantial, and the resulting authority needs to be drafted with care to survive constitutional challenges while providing the actual operational capability the program requires.

Cross-RC reliability coordination is more complex than for the non-RTO pilots because PJM is a major NERC reliability coordinator with substantial operational responsibilities. The protocols for who has dispatch authority during reliability events, how the HVDC link responds to PJM’s emergency procedures versus TVA’s, and how NERC contingency standards are met across the seam require detailed specification. The federal anchor legislation should provide NERC with explicit authority to develop coordination standards for federal anchor transmission, with FERC oversight ensuring the standards are operationally workable.

Yellow Creek to Central Mississippi: Cross-RTO Seam Architecture, MISO Edition

The Yellow Creek to Central Mississippi line replicates the Clinch River architecture with MISO South as the relevant RTO rather than PJM. The structural pattern is the same: federally-owned HVDC line, TVA operational dispatch, MISO scheduling the AC integration at the receiving converter station, fixed-schedule firm delivery rather than LMP-integrated dispatch.

MISO’s tariff has somewhat different provisions than PJM’s, and the specific accommodations needed differ in detail. MISO South in particular has different historical seams arrangements than PJM, with the legacy Entergy bilateral relationships still influencing how cross-seam transmission is handled. The legislative lift is broadly comparable to the Clinch River case — federal anchor transmission category, MISO tariff revisions, federal eminent domain authority, cross-RC coordination protocols — but the specific MISO tariff development would proceed in parallel with rather than identical to the PJM development.

There is a modest political case that the Yellow Creek pilot is FERC-easier than the Clinch River pilot because MISO is generally considered easier to coordinate with than PJM, with less concentrated market power among receiving utilities and somewhat more flexible scheduling procedures. But the legislative architecture required is essentially the same, and the cumulative federal regulatory framework needs to accommodate both PJM and MISO interactions through consistent provisions rather than divergent ones.

Cumulative Legislative and Regulatory Package

The transmission technology and ownership architecture developed in this document requires a substantial federal legislative package that supplements the Federal Anchor Generation Authority described in the main proposal. The key provisions are:

A defined federal anchor transmission category within the Federal Anchor Generation Authority statute, specifying the federal anchor authority’s ownership of cross-jurisdictional segments, the operational delegation framework to anchor POU operators, the segmented ownership architecture across federal authority, anchor POU, and destination IOU segments, and the receiving converter station structure with federal majority ownership and IOU minority economic participation.

Federal eminent domain authority for federal anchor transmission, structured through a renewed Section 216 that is substantive enough to clear hostile state action. The authority should include explicit eminent domain provisions, expedited judicial review, and clear delineation of the scope of federal versus state authority over anchor program assets during construction and operation.

Preemption of destination-state CPCN authority for federal anchor transmission during the build phase, with state authority limited to defined safety, environmental, and emergency-response matters during operations. This is contested but essential — without meaningful preemption, the destination-state PUCs in Virginia, North Carolina, and other states could effectively veto the federal anchor program through state-level regulatory action.

FERC tariff authority for federal anchor transmission, with the federal anchor authority authorized to file negotiated-rate or cost-of-service rate tariffs under Section 205. The authority should include explicit direction to affected RTOs (PJM, MISO, NYISO, SPP, ISO-NE, CAISO) to file tariff revisions accommodating federal anchor transmission within their footprints, with FERC oversight ensuring the revisions are non-discriminatory and operationally workable.

NERC reliability coordination provisions specifying how federal anchor transmission is registered, how operational responsibilities are allocated across federal authority, anchor POU, and destination utility, and how cross-RC coordination protocols are developed. The provisions should give NERC explicit authority to develop coordination standards for the federal anchor transmission category, with FERC oversight.

TVA Act amendments expanding TVA’s debt cap to accommodate the home-jurisdiction segment financing alongside the plant acquisition financing, and providing the narrow fence carve-out for wholesale sales of federally-anchored generation that the main proposal already contemplates. The amendments do not need to expand TVA’s territorial authority for transmission ownership because the segmented ownership architecture handles cross-jurisdictional ownership through the federal anchor authority rather than through TVA.

This is a substantial legislative package, but it is internally coherent and matches the structural design of the anchor program rather than overlaying a patchwork on existing authorities. Each provision serves a specific function in the program architecture, and the provisions together form an integrated framework that FERC, NERC, the affected RTOs, and the destination-state regulatory authorities can implement consistently across the Phase 1 pilots and the Phase 2 expansion.

Conclusion

The transmission technology and ownership architecture developed in this document specifies the structural choices that the main proposal leaves at a higher level of generality. The recommendations land on a fleet portfolio that is three-quarters HVDC and one-quarter AC, with HVDC selected for the longer pilots and for any pilot that crosses an RTO seam, and with the AC pilot designed to accommodate future HVDC retrofit if conditions warrant. The segment ownership architecture has the anchor POU owning the home-jurisdiction segment, the federal anchor authority owning the cross-jurisdictional segment including the receiving converter station, and the destination IOU owning the downstream AC leg with a minority economic stake in the receiving converter station. Operational dispatch of the federal segments is delegated to the anchor POU under long-term operating agreements, preserving dispatch coherence for the anchor POU while avoiding the legal lift of expanding the POU’s territorial authority.

The architecture is more complex than the Pacific Intertie precedent it draws on, reflecting the more constrained political environment in which the anchor program operates. The principal complications — federal middle segments, separated economic and operational ownership of receiving converter stations, the long-term operational delegation framework — are responses to specific structural challenges (cross-jurisdictional ownership, IOU coalition equity, autonomy preservation under contested regulatory conditions) that the Intertie did not face. The underlying logic of the Intertie nonetheless holds: cost allocation is settled by ownership, segments meet at clean boundaries, each owner has a defined institutional stake.

The FERC regulatory architecture is substantial but achievable. Each pilot makes different demands on the existing regulatory framework, with the cross-RTO pilots (Clinch River and Yellow Creek) requiring the most novel arrangements. The legislative package required to enable the architecture is internally coherent and matches the structural design of the anchor program. The political fight over the federal preemption provisions and the FERC tariff direction will be substantial, but the substantive case for the architecture is defensible and the structural integrity of the program depends on the architecture being implemented as designed rather than diluted through bargaining toward simpler but structurally weaker alternatives.

The honest assessment is that some version of the architecture developed here is likely necessary for the anchor program to actually deliver its core promise: substantial new interregional clean firm capacity, anchored to publicly-owned generation, operated by publicly-owned utilities, with the structural autonomy of public power preserved across multi-decade asset lives. Simpler architectures (pure AC, simple Pacific Intertie ownership patterns, full delegation to destination IOUs) would be politically easier to enact but would surrender the structural protections that make the program meaningful for public power’s long-term institutional position. The architecture described here is a reasonable compromise between political achievability and structural integrity, and it provides the technical foundation for the political analysis developed in the companion document on opposition forces and risk mitigation.
